Literature Review Workshop

PSIR220 · In-Class Activity · Week 8 Session 1

What We're Doing Today

A literature review is not a list of summaries. It's a conversation organised by schools of thought. Today you'll practise the three hardest skills:

  • Sorting sources into schools of thought
  • Writing umbrella sentences that introduce each school
  • Finding the gap that justifies your research

Then you'll apply these skills to your own annotated bibliography.

1The Sort

Below are 10 source summaries on the topic: "Why do some peace agreements hold while others collapse?"

Your job: read each one and decide which school of thought it belongs to. Don't overthink it — go with your instinct, then we'll discuss.

Fortna (2004)
Using a dataset of 48 ceasefires (1989–1999), Fortna finds that peace agreements with strong enforcement mechanisms — peacekeeping troops, demilitarised zones, third-party guarantees — are statistically more durable. The key variable is credible commitment: when parties can't cheat without detection, peace holds.
Autesserre (2010)
Based on two years of ethnographic fieldwork in the DRC, Autesserre argues that international peacebuilders consistently misread local conflicts as extensions of the national war. They impose top-down frameworks that ignore village-level land disputes, ethnic rivalries, and everyday violence — which is why peace "holds" nationally but collapses locally.
Walter (2002)
Walter analyses 41 civil wars and argues that the core obstacle to peace is the security dilemma during demobilisation. Without third-party security guarantees, combatants rationally refuse to disarm. Her statistical model shows that third-party enforcement is the single strongest predictor of successful implementation.
Paris (2004)
Paris argues that the liberal peacebuilding model — rapid democratisation and market liberalisation — has repeatedly destabilised post-conflict societies. The international community imposes a template that serves Western institutional interests rather than addressing the structural inequalities that caused the war.
Mac Ginty (2011)
Drawing on cases from Bosnia, Sri Lanka, and Lebanon, Mac Ginty argues that peace is not simply "implemented" — it is negotiated daily by local communities who blend international frameworks with indigenous conflict-resolution practices. What outsiders call "spoiling" is often local agency resisting an imported model.
Doyle & Sambanis (2006)
Analysing 124 post-conflict cases with a mixed-methods design, Doyle and Sambanis find that peacebuilding success depends on the interaction of local capacities (economic development, prior democracy) and international capacities (UN missions, aid). Neither alone is sufficient; the combination is what matters for statistical prediction.
Richmond (2011)
Richmond argues that mainstream peacebuilding scholarship reproduces a colonial logic: Western actors define what "peace" means, design the institutions, and evaluate success on their own terms. Local populations are treated as recipients, not agents. A truly emancipatory peace must start from below.
Björkdahl & Höglund (2013)
Through comparative case studies of Bosnia and Cambodia, they argue that "peace" means fundamentally different things to different actors in a post-conflict setting. International monitors define peace as absence of violence; local women define it as freedom from domestic abuse and economic survival. These competing meanings shape whether peace is experienced as real.
Chandler (2010)
Chandler contends that international statebuilding in places like Bosnia and Kosovo has created "phantom states" — formally sovereign entities that are actually governed by external actors. This arrangement serves the geopolitical interests of Western powers while denying genuine self-determination to local populations.
Hegre & Nygård (2015)
Using a global statistical model covering 1970–2009, Hegre and Nygård forecast conflict recurrence based on structural variables: GDP per capita, population size, regime type, and neighbourhood effects. They find that economic development is the strongest long-term predictor of sustained peace, independent of agreement design.

Answer Key

Positivist / Rationalist

Fortna, Walter, Doyle & Sambanis, Hegre & Nygård — These share a commitment to measurable variables, large-N datasets, statistical models, and testable hypotheses about what causes peace to hold or fail.

Interpretivist / Constructivist

Autesserre, Mac Ginty, Björkdahl & Höglund — These emphasise meaning, local perspectives, ethnographic methods, and how "peace" is understood differently by different actors. They resist reducing peace to variables.

Critical / Postcolonial

Paris, Richmond, Chandler — These expose power dynamics in peacebuilding itself: who designs peace, whose model gets imposed, and how liberal peacebuilding can reproduce colonial hierarchies.

Note: Paris is debatable — some classify him as a liberal critic within the positivist tradition. Mac Ginty also has critical elements. That ambiguity is the point: schools of thought have fuzzy boundaries, and your job is to justify your classification.

2Write Umbrella Sentences

An umbrella sentence introduces a school of thought in your literature review. It tells the reader: "Here is a group of scholars who share an approach, and this is what they collectively argue."

Bad example: "Fortna (2004) argues that enforcement mechanisms matter. Walter (2002) also looks at security guarantees."

Good example: "The dominant explanation in the rationalist literature attributes peace agreement durability to the design of credible commitment mechanisms — particularly third-party enforcement, demilitarised zones, and power-sharing provisions (Fortna 2004; Walter 2002; Doyle & Sambanis 2006)."

Your Turn

Write an umbrella sentence for each of the three schools. Use the sources from the sort exercise.

⏱ 10 minutes

3Find the Gap

You've now mapped three schools of thought on peace agreement durability. Each school explains something the others miss — but all three together still leave questions unanswered.

A gap can be:

  • Empirical: a case, region, or time period nobody has studied
  • Theoretical: a mechanism or interaction the frameworks don't capture
  • Methodological: a question never studied with a particular approach

Your Turn

Write 2–3 sentences identifying a gap in this literature. What has the debate missed?

⏱ 8 minutes

Example Gaps

Empirical: "While the rationalist literature has extensively studied UN-mediated agreements, it has largely ignored peace processes led by regional organisations such as the African Union or ASEAN, which operate under different institutional logics."

Theoretical: "The constructivist emphasis on local meaning-making and the critical focus on power hierarchies have developed in parallel but rarely engage each other. How local actors navigate — and sometimes exploit — the power asymmetries that critical scholars describe remains undertheorised."

Methodological: "The quantitative literature can identify which variables correlate with peace durability but cannot trace the mechanisms through which agreement provisions actually change combatant behaviour on the ground. The interpretivist literature provides this depth but for single cases. A structured comparison applying process tracing across a small-N set of cases would bridge this divide."
