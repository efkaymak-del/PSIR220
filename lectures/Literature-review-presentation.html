What a Literature Review Actually Is

"A literature review is an argument about the literature that justifies the selection of the question you want to answer and the basic approach to answering it." — Knopf & McMenamin (2008)

It IS…

A scholarly conversation — you walk the reader through what different camps believe, where they agree, where they clash, and what nobody has addressed yet. You organise by argument, not by author.

It is NOT…

A book-report parade: "Author A says X. Author B says Y. Author C says Z." That's an annotated bibliography in disguise. If each paragraph discusses only one source, something is wrong.

Baglione's Core Insight (Ch. 4)

Group your sources into schools of thought — clusters of scholars who share a theoretical logic. Then show how these schools debate each other. Your literature review is a map of that debate, and you are the cartographer.

From Annotated Bibliography → Literature Review

Same sources, completely different product.

Annotated Bibliography

Unit of analysis: individual source

Organisation: source by source

Voice: descriptive — "This article argues…"

Purpose: show you've read and evaluated each source

Literature Review

Unit of analysis: school of thought / argument cluster

Organisation: thematic — grouped by shared logic

Voice: analytical — "Realists contend… but constructivists counter…"

Purpose: map the debate and locate the gap your research fills

The key move: you stop treating each source as a stand-alone entry and start weaving multiple sources into a single paragraph around a shared claim. Instead of ten isolated paragraphs, you produce a three- or four-section narrative.

The Four-Section Structure

Your literature review follows this arc.

Section 1 — The Landscape (100–200 words · 1.5 pts)

Frame the broad puzzle or debate. Why does this question matter? What makes it contentious or unresolved? Set the scene without diving into specific authors yet.

Section 2 — The Schools of Thought (600–800 words · 4 pts)

This is the heart. Group your sources into 2–3 schools. For each school, explain: the shared argument, the evidence or methods used, areas of agreement within the school, and points of disagreement between schools. Your own paradigm track gets the most detailed treatment.

Section 3 — The Gap (150–250 words · 2 pts)

What does the debate miss? This could be an empirical gap (unstudied cases, missing data), a theoretical gap (logic that doesn't account for X), or a methodological gap (everyone uses the same method and no one tries Y).

Section 4 — Your Contribution (100–200 words · 1.5 pts)

State clearly how your research fills the gap. This paragraph is a bridge to the Research Design assignment coming next.

Organising by Schools of Thought

Baglione calls these "camps." Knopf & McMenamin call them "schools." Same idea.

How to identify a school

A school of thought is a cluster of scholars who share a causal logic — they agree on the type of mechanism that drives outcomes, even if they disagree on details. Ask: "Do these authors explain the outcome using the same kind of reasoning?"

Example: Why do states comply with human-rights treaties?

School A — Rationalist: States comply when material incentives (aid conditionality, trade access) make non-compliance costly (Hafner-Burton 2005; Simmons 2009).

School B — Constructivist: Compliance follows socialisation — states internalise norms through sustained interaction with treaty bodies and peer pressure (Risse, Ropp & Sikkink 1999; Finnemore & Sikkink 1998).

School C — Critical: "Compliance" is itself a discursive construction; powerful states define what counts as compliance to maintain hegemonic order (Mutua 2001; Doty 1996).

Notice: Each school is defined by its logic, not just its conclusion. Multiple authors are grouped under one heading. That's the move from AB to LR.

Five Writing Moves for the Schools Section

Use these sentence-level strategies to weave sources together.

1. The Umbrella Statement

Open a paragraph by naming the school's shared claim, then cite several authors together.

Example:"A prominent strand of the literature attributes treaty compliance to rational cost–benefit calculations. Hafner-Burton (2005) and Simmons (2009) both demonstrate that states respond to material incentives such as aid conditionality and trade access."

2. The Internal Nuance

Show that scholars within a school still disagree on specifics.

Example:"While Simmons (2009) emphasises domestic litigation as the enforcement mechanism, Hafner-Burton (2005) points to naming-and-shaming by international organisations — suggesting that even within the rationalist camp, the precise pathway remains contested."

3. The Bridge / Pivot

Transition between schools by highlighting the disagreement.

Example:"However, constructivists challenge this materialist logic. Risse et al. (1999) argue that compliance is not primarily driven by incentives but by a process of norm internalisation…"

4. The Synthesis

Note where two schools surprisingly agree.

Example:"Despite their theoretical differences, both rationalists and constructivists agree that domestic institutions mediate the effect of international pressure — a point neither school has fully explored."

5. The Gap Sentence

Signal what's missing — this is your setup for Section 3.

Example:"What remains largely unexamined in both accounts is the role of regional organisations, which operate at an intermediate level between domestic and global dynamics."

Common Fatal Flaws in Literature Reviews

Avoid these — they are the most frequent reasons for low marks.

1. The Book-Report Parade

"Author A says X. Author B says Y." Each paragraph = one source. No grouping, no comparison, no argument. This is an annotated bibliography with the headings removed.

Fix: Merge sources that share a logic into a single paragraph under an umbrella statement.

2. Strawmanning Rivals

Dismissing competing schools in a sentence ("Realists ignore identity") without engaging their strongest arguments. Knopf & McMenamin: "engage with the most charitable interpretation."

Fix: Present each school's best case, then show specifically what it fails to explain.

3. The Ghost Gap

Declaring a gap that isn't actually grounded in the review. "No one has studied X" — but you haven't shown why that matters or how it follows from the debate you just described.

Fix: The gap must emerge logically from the clash between schools.

4. No Transitions

Jumping from one school to another without signalling the relationship. The reader can't follow the argument's arc.

Fix: Use pivot phrases: "However," "In contrast," "Building on this logic," "Despite this consensus."

5. Over-Quoting

Filling the review with long block quotes instead of paraphrasing and synthesising. This signals you haven't processed the material.

Fix: Paraphrase in your own words; quote only when exact phrasing matters (definitions, contested terms).

6. Losing Your Question

Discussing fascinating tangents that don't connect back to your research question. As Knopf & McMenamin note: content should be included on a "need-to-know" basis, not a "look-at-what-I've-read" basis.

Fix: Every paragraph should visibly serve the question.

Tips by Paradigm Track

Your track shapes how you write the review — but you must still engage the others.

Strategist Positivist Track

Your schools will often be defined by competing independent variables: School A says X causes Y; School B says Z causes Y. Highlight the evidence each side marshals (datasets, case comparisons, statistical methods). Your gap might be an untested variable, an under-studied region, or a missing interaction effect.

Storyteller Interpretivist Track

Your schools may differ in how they interpret meaning: School A reads the event through identity; School B through institutional culture. Emphasise the interpretive frameworks and primary sources each camp draws on. Your gap might be a silenced voice, an under-explored archive, or a context no one has applied the framework to.

Disruptor Critical Track

Your schools may vary by whose power structures they foreground: School A focuses on class, School B on race, School C on gender. Emphasise the normative stakes and silences in each camp's analysis. Your gap might be an intersection of oppressions, a geographic blind spot, or a discourse that hasn't been deconstructed.

Golden rule: No matter your track, you must present at least one rival school fairly and seriously. The literature review is where you prove you understand the debate — not just your side of it.

Exemplary Mini–Literature Review

Topic: Why do some states comply with international human-rights treaties while others do not?
Paradigm: Interpretivist · Storyteller  |  ~1,100 words

Section 1 — The Landscape

The gap between the formal ratification of international human-rights treaties and their actual implementation remains one of the most persistent puzzles in international relations. More than 170 states have ratified the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights, yet systematic violations continue in many signatory countries (Hathaway 2002). This paradox has generated a rich scholarly debate spanning rationalist, constructivist, and critical perspectives — each offering a distinct account of why the gap exists and what might close it. Understanding this debate is essential not only for theory but for the practical design of international monitoring mechanisms.

Section 2 — The Schools of Thought

A prominent strand of the literature explains treaty compliance through a rationalist lens, treating states as strategic actors responding to material costs and benefits. Simmons (2009) demonstrates that treaties are most effective in states with independent domestic courts, because citizens can use treaty provisions as a legal resource to hold governments accountable. Hafner-Burton (2005) focuses on a different mechanism — preferential trade agreements that include human-rights clauses — arguing that economic incentives compel compliance where reputational pressure alone would not. Despite their shared materialist assumptions, these scholars disagree about the precise pathway: Simmons privileges domestic-level litigation, while Hafner-Burton emphasises inter-state conditionality. What unites the rationalist camp, however, is the conviction that compliance is ultimately driven by incentive structures rather than by changes in state identity or belief.

A second body of scholarship rejects this incentive-based logic and instead explains compliance through norm socialisation. Risse, Ropp, and Sikkink (1999) propose a five-phase "spiral model" in which states move from denial to tactical concessions to genuine norm internalisation through sustained pressure from transnational advocacy networks. Finnemore and Sikkink (1998) complement this account with their concept of a "norm cascade," arguing that once a critical mass of states adopts a norm, peer conformity accelerates adoption by holdouts. The constructivist camp thus locates the causal engine not in material costs but in social learning and identity transformation — states comply because they come to see human-rights adherence as constitutive of what it means to be a "legitimate" member of international society. Within this school, however, there is debate about how deep internalisation must be; Risse et al. (1999) acknowledge that some states stall at the "tactical concession" stage, suggesting that socialisation is not always complete.

Critical scholars challenge both of the preceding accounts by questioning the very categories through which compliance is defined and measured. Mutua (2001) argues that the international human-rights regime reflects a Western "savages–victims–saviours" narrative that positions Global South states as inherently deficient. From this perspective, "non-compliance" may in part be an artefact of standards designed without meaningful participation from the communities they claim to protect. Doty (1996) extends this critique through discourse analysis, showing how representational practices in policy documents construct certain states as "failed" or "rogue," thereby justifying interventionist compliance mechanisms. The critical camp does not necessarily deny that material incentives or socialisation processes exist, but insists that these operate within power-laden discursive structures that mainstream scholarship takes for granted.

Section 3 — The Gap

Despite the richness of this three-cornered debate, a notable gap persists regarding the role of regional organisations as mediators of compliance. Rationalists focus on bilateral conditionality or domestic courts; constructivists emphasise global-level norms and transnational networks; critical scholars interrogate the universalist framing of the global regime. Yet in practice, compliance pressure is often channelled through regional bodies — the European Court of Human Rights, the African Commission on Human and Peoples' Rights, the Inter-American system — whose institutional logics, membership norms, and power dynamics differ markedly from both global institutions and bilateral relationships. Simmons (2009) briefly notes regional variation but does not theorise it; Risse et al. (1999) mention the European case as "most likely" for socialisation but do not systematically compare across regional contexts. No study has yet examined how the design of regional human-rights mechanisms interacts with both material incentives and norm diffusion to produce divergent compliance outcomes.

Section 4 — Your Contribution

This study proposes to fill this gap by conducting an interpretivist comparative analysis of compliance processes in three regional human-rights systems — the European, Inter-American, and African — focusing on how each institution's discourse of "legitimate statehood" shapes the socialisation pathway that Risse et al. (1999) describe. By examining treaty-body reports, state submissions, and NGO shadow reports as primary texts, the study will trace how compliance is narrated and negotiated at the regional level, rather than simply measured as a binary outcome. This approach speaks directly to the upcoming research design, which will specify case-selection criteria and the interpretive method in detail.

Note: This is a model — your topic and paradigm will differ, but the structure (landscape → schools → gap → contribution) and writing moves (umbrella statements, internal nuance, pivots, gap sentences) should be the same.

Finding the Gap: Three Categories

Knopf & McMenamin (2008) suggest summarising the literature in terms of:

1. Consensus

What do most scholars agree on? This is the "conventional wisdom." Don't accept it uncritically — probe for potential weaknesses.

2. Debate

Where do scholars disagree, and why? Different assumptions? Different data? Different methods? Pinpointing the source of disagreement helps you target your research.

3. Gaps

What has nobody examined? Unstudied cases, overlooked perspectives, missing theoretical connections, or untried methods. This is where your contribution lives.

Your literature review should touch all three. The Landscape can note consensus; the Schools section maps the debate; the Gap section names what's missing; and the Contribution section claims that territory.

Quick Reference: Dos & Don'ts

DO

  • Group 2–4 sources in one paragraph under a shared claim
  • Use pivot phrases between schools ("However," "In contrast," "Building on…")
  • Present rivals charitably — steelman, then critique
  • Ground the gap in the actual debate you just described
  • Write in your own voice — paraphrase, don't quote-dump
  • Cite consistently (APA or Chicago Author–Date)
  • Re-read Baglione Ch. 4 before starting

DON'T

  • Write one paragraph per source (book-report parade)
  • Dismiss rival schools in a single sentence
  • Invent a gap that doesn't follow from the review
  • Include sources that don't connect to your question
  • Use long block quotes as filler
  • Forget the reference list
  • Let AI write it — use it only for grammar/structure checks

Summary

1. A literature review is an argument about a debate — not a list of summaries.

2. Organise by schools of thought, not by individual authors.

3. Follow the four-section arc: Landscape → Schools → Gap → Contribution.

4. Present rivals charitably; the strongest critique is one that takes the other side seriously.

5. The gap must emerge from the debate — it's not something you invent; it's something the debate reveals.

6. Use the Literature Review Builder app to draft, check quality, and export.
