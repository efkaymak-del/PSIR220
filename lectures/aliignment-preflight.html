The Core Concept
A research design is not a collection of parts.
It is an argument that the parts fit together.
Baglione calls this "Making Your Plan and Protecting Yourself from Criticism" (Ch. 7). KKV call it "internal consistency." We call it alignment.

When It Works
An Aligned Design

Example — Strategist Track

RQ: "Why did populist parties gain more seats after the 2008 crisis in Italy than in Germany?"
Track: Strategist (Positivist) — seeks to explain variation
Lit Review: Organised by schools — demand-side (economic anxiety) vs. supply-side (party strategy) vs. institutional (electoral system)
Pathway: Economic shock (X) → voter insecurity activates anti-elite sentiment (M) → populist vote share (Y)
Method: Most Similar Systems Design
N: 2 (Italy, Germany) — similar economies, different electoral outcomes

Every piece reinforces the others. A reviewer can follow the logic from question to cases without a single jump.
When It Breaks
Common Misalignment Patterns

Track ≠ Question

You declared Interpretivist (Storyteller) but your question asks "to what extent" — a Strategist question seeking measurable variation.

Fix: Reword the question or switch tracks.

Track ≠ Sources

You declared Disruptor but your bibliography is entirely positivist — Mearsheimer, Waltz, regression studies. No critical theory texts at all.

Fix: Add track-appropriate literature or change your declaration.

Method ≠ N-Size

You chose process tracing (a single-case method) but listed 15 countries. Or you chose large-N regression but have data on 4 cases.

Fix: Adjust N to match the method's logic, or pick a different method.

Pathway ≠ Track

You declared Disruptor but your causal engine is X → M → Y with measurable variables and no mention of power or discourse.

Fix: Rebuild the pathway using your paradigm's logic of explanation.

Making Your Plan and Protecting
Yourself from Criticism
Baglione uses two analogies. The courtroom analogy: your research design is a legal brief — it must convince a skeptical judge that your method of gathering evidence is sound. The medical analogy: your design is a treatment protocol — if the diagnosis (question) is wrong, the treatment (method) will fail no matter how well-administered.
"The research design is where you make the case that your plan for answering the question is logical, that the evidence you will collect is the right kind, and that you have anticipated the objections a critic would raise."
— Baglione, Ch. 7
Translation: alignment is not optional. It is the core of what makes a design credible.
The Broader Debate
What the Methods Scholars Say

KKV — Designing Social Inquiry

A research design must maximise observable implications of your theory. The more testable predictions your design generates, the stronger it is. Internal consistency means every design choice increases your ability to distinguish your theory from rivals.

Strategists: this is your Bible. Your design must generate variation, control for alternatives, and produce replicable evidence.

Brady & Collier — Rethinking Social Inquiry

KKV's framework is too narrow. Qualitative research has its own standards of inference — process tracing, causal-process observations, and within-case analysis are not lesser versions of regression. Alignment means matching your evidence to the logic of your paradigm, not forcing everything into one template.

Storytellers and Disruptors: your methods have their own rules. Alignment means following those rules, not apologising for not running a regression.
Both sides agree: design choices must be justified, not arbitrary. The Alignment Checker helps you do exactly that.
Three Tracks, Three Design Logics
What "good alignment" looks like on each track

Strategist

Question: Why / To what extent
Pathway: IV → Mechanism → DV
Sources: Quantitative studies, datasets, realist/liberal canon
Method: MSSD, MDSD, large-N, QCA
N: 2–5 (comparative) or 50+ (statistical)

Key test: Can you state a falsifiable hypothesis?

Storyteller

Question: How / What meaning
Pathway: Context → Interpretation → Outcome
Sources: Qualitative studies, primary documents, constructivist theory
Method: Process tracing, ethnography, discourse analysis
N: 1 (deep case) or 2–3 (comparative process tracing)

Key test: Can you access the actors' meaning-making?

Disruptor

Question: Whose interests / What is hidden
Pathway: Power → Discourse → Reality
Sources: Critical theory, postcolonial texts, primary discourse material
Method: CDA, genealogy, deconstruction
N: 1 (corpus or archive)

Key test: Can you show how discourse constructs "reality"?
Lessons from Your Bibliographies
The Three Most Common Alignment Failures in This Class
1

Paradigm–Bibliography Mismatch

Several of you declared Interpretivist or Disruptor but filled your bibliography with Positivist sources. Your literature should reflect your track — not just the first ten results on Google Scholar.

2

No Puzzle in the Research Question

A good research question contains a surprise — something that shouldn't happen according to theory, but does. "Why do states go to war?" is not a puzzle. "Why did democracies fail to prevent genocide in Rwanda?" is.

3

Shallow Annotations That Dodge Evaluation

Many entries summarised the source but never evaluated it. "This article is relevant" is not evaluation. "This article uses survey data from 2015, which may not capture post-crisis attitudes" is.

The Alignment Checker will flag these automatically. Your job today is to fix them.

The Checklist
Six Steps to Alignment
1

Research Question

Is it a why/how question? Does it contain a puzzle? Is it scoped to a time, place, and set of cases?

2

Paradigm Track

Does the question match the track's logic? A Strategist needs variation; a Storyteller needs meaning; a Disruptor needs discourse.

3

Causal Pathway

Is X different from Y? Is the mechanism specified, not just asserted? Does it fit the track's causal logic?

4

Method

Does the method match the paradigm? Can it actually test your mechanism? Is it feasible in one semester?

5

N-Size & Cases

Does the N match the method? Are cases selected for analytic reasons, not convenience? Is there variation in the dependent variable?

6

Ethics & Access

Can you actually obtain the data? Do you need ethical clearance? Is the timeline realistic?

Diagnosis
The Traffic-Light System

Green — Go

This element is aligned with the rest of your design. No action needed. Proceed with confidence.

Example: Strategist with a "why" question that specifies variation across cases.

Amber — Explain Yourself

Not a fatal flaw, but an unusual combination. You must justify this choice in your design paper. If you can't justify it, it's actually a red.

Example: Storyteller using QCA (mid-N) — possible but must explain why not process tracing.

Red — Grounded

A fundamental contradiction. Do not submit your design until this is resolved. Something must change — the question, the track, the method, or the cases.

Example: Disruptor running a 200-country regression. The method contradicts the paradigm entirely.
Goal: all greens, or greens with justified ambers. Any red = grounded until fixed.
Live Diagnosis
A Design with Three Red Flags

Student X (fictional)

RQ: "Is climate change bad for developing countries?"
Track: Interpretivist (Storyteller)
Pathway: Climate change (X) → economic damage (M) → underdevelopment (Y)
Method: Large-N regression, 120 countries
Sources: World Bank datasets, IPCC reports, Nordhaus (2013)

Red: Yes/No Question

"Is climate change bad?" can be answered with one word. No puzzle, no variation.

Rewrite: "Why do some small island states adapt to climate change more successfully than others?"

Red: Track ≠ Method

Declared Storyteller but using large-N regression — a Strategist method that contradicts interpretive logic.

Either switch to Strategist, or switch method to process tracing on 1–2 island states.

Red: Track ≠ Sources

Entirely positivist bibliography (datasets, IPCC, economics). No interpretivist theory — no Geertz, no constructivism, no thick description.

Add context-specific case studies, anthropological sources on climate adaptation, local voices.

Amber: Tautology Risk

X (climate change) and Y (underdevelopment) may overlap — economic damage is part of what defines underdevelopment.

Separate X and Y conceptually. Define underdevelopment independently of climate impact.
After Repair
The Same Student, Aligned

Student X — Revised Design

RQ: "How do small island developing states construct 'climate resilience' in national policy narratives — and why do Fiji and Tuvalu frame it so differently?"
Track: Storyteller (Interpretivist)
Pathway: Shared climate vulnerability (context) → different national identity narratives frame "resilience" differently (interpretation) → divergent policy responses (outcome)
Method: Comparative process tracing, discourse analysis of policy documents
N: 2 (Fiji, Tuvalu)
Sources: Barnett & Campbell (2010), Farbotko (2010), official adaptation plans, parliamentary speeches, Geertz (1973) as interpretive method anchor

Every link connects. Question → Track → Sources → Pathway → Method → Cases. All green.

Individual Pre-Flight Check
30 minutes
1

Open the Alignment Checker

Use the web tool on your device, or the paper worksheet — your choice. Both cover the same six steps.

2

Work through all six steps honestly

Don't write what sounds good — write what you've actually committed to. If a section is blank, that's a finding.

3

Run the diagnostic (or self-diagnose on paper)

Count your greens, ambers, and reds. Fill in the Design Summary at the bottom — you'll need it next week.

4

If you hit red flags, start fixing now

Rewrite the question, swap a method, add a source. Don't wait — the Research Design is due in three weeks.

Workshop — Part 2
Peer Pre-Flight Review
30 minutes
1

Pair up and exchange Design Summaries

Share your screen or swap worksheets. Read your partner's summary in silence for 3 minutes.

2

Spot what the checker missed

Look for alignment problems that the self-check didn't catch. Is the mechanism actually testable? Is N realistic? Are the cases comparable?

3

Fill in the Peer Review section

Identify the strongest part, the weakest link, and one specific revision you'd recommend.

4

Discuss for 5 minutes per partner

Be honest but constructive. This is practice for the Saboteurs Workshop — where your partner's job is to break your design.
