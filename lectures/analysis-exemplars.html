Why Exemplars?

You've learned the theory of analysis — test observable implications, trace causal chains, map discursive patterns. But what does it look like on the page?

"The best way to learn to write a research paper is to read research papers — not to summarize them, but to dissect them."
— Baglione, Ch. 8

Today we dissect seven published studies. For each, we ask:

  1. What was the hypothesis or framework?
  2. What evidence did they present?
  3. How did they structure the analysis?
  4. What was the verdict — and how honest were they about it?
  5. What made it convincing (or not)?

Exemplar 1 · Strategist

Putnam — Making Democracy Work (1993)

The Setup

Question: Why do some Italian regional governments perform better than others?

Hypothesis: Regions with stronger civic traditions (civic engagement, trust, associational life) will have better-performing institutions.

Method: Comparative analysis of 20 Italian regions, using an institutional performance index + a civic community index, spanning 1970s–1990s.

Observable Implications:

  • OI-1: Regions with more associations, newspaper readership, and voter turnout should have higher institutional performance scores.
  • OI-2: The correlation should hold even when controlling for economic development.
  • OI-3: Historical civic traditions (1860–1920) should predict 1980s performance — a 100-year lag test.
Exemplar 1 · Strategist

Putnam — The Analysis

What He Found

TestEvidenceResult
OI-1: Civic life → performancer = 0.92 between civic community index and institutional performance index across 20 regions✅ Strong
OI-2: Controlling for wealthCivic traditions predict performance even after controlling for economic development; wealth alone does not predict performance✅ Holds
OI-3: 100-year lag"Figure 5.4 presents the correlation between institutional performance in the 1980s and civic traditions in 1860–1920. The pattern is stark."✅ Dramatic

The Rival

Economic modernization theory: wealth, not civic culture, drives performance. Putnam tests it directly — and wealth fails as a predictor when civic traditions are included.

Verdict: Hypothesis supported across all three tests.
Exemplar 1 · Strategist

Putnam — Why It Convinces

What makes this analysis strong:

  • Multiple observable implications — not one test, but three, each progressively harder
  • The rival is tested with the same data — economic development is not dismissed; it's measured and compared
  • The 100-year lag — this is a creativity move. If civic traditions in 1870 predict institutional performance in 1980, the causal story deepens dramatically
  • Quantitative evidence presented clearly — correlations, scatterplots, named regions

What critics attacked:

  • Tarrow (1996): the direction of causation may be reversed — good institutions may produce civic engagement, not the other way around
  • The 20 regions may not be independent observations (spatial correlation)

Lesson for your CQ4: More OIs = stronger design. Test your rival, don't just name it. And look for the creative test — the one that would be really hard for the rival to explain.

Exemplar 2 · Strategist

Norris & Inglehart — Cultural Backlash (2019)

The Setup

Question: What explains the rise of authoritarian populist parties across Western democracies?

Hypothesis: Cultural backlash — once-dominant groups (older, less educated, male, religious, rural) react against progressive cultural change (gender equality, immigration, multiculturalism, LGBTQ rights).

Rival: Economic insecurity thesis — globalization's "losers" turn to populism.

Method: Large-N regression using European Social Survey and World Values Survey across 30+ countries.

Exemplar 2 · Strategist

Norris & Inglehart — The Analysis

What They Found

TestEvidenceResult
Cultural values predict populist voteAuthoritarian values (anti-immigration, traditional gender attitudes) are significant predictors of voting for populist parties✅ Supported
Economic variablesIncome and unemployment have weaker, less consistent effects than cultural attitudes⚠️ Weak
Generational cohortOlder cohorts are more authoritarian — consistent with "backlash" against decades of liberalization✅ Consistent
Cross-nationalPattern holds across 30+ countries — not country-specific✅ Robust
Verdict: Cultural backlash thesis supported; economic thesis partially supported but weaker. The authors note both factors interact.
Exemplar 2 · Strategist

Norris & Inglehart — Honesty About Limits

What makes this analysis instructive:

  • They don't claim the rival is wrong — they show it's weaker. "We find the most consistent evidence supporting the cultural backlash thesis" ≠ "economics doesn't matter."
  • They use "most consistent" — careful language, not "proves."
  • Critics responded (e.g., Oesch 2008; Rovny & Rovny 2017) — showing that economics matters more in some countries. This is normal. Science is a conversation, not a verdict.

Lesson for your CQ4: "Partially supported" is a perfectly respectable verdict. The key is: can you show which part is supported and which isn't? That's analytical precision.

Exemplar 3 · Strategist

Geddes — Paradigms and Sand Castles (2003)

When the analysis refutes — and that's the contribution.

The Setup

Question: Why do existing theories of democratization produce conflicting findings?

Argument: The problem isn't theory — it's selection bias. Scholars study only cases that democratized, then find "causes" that are actually artifacts of selecting on the dependent variable.

Evidence: Reviews large-N studies of democratization and shows that when you include countries that didn't democratize, the patterns disappear.

Exemplar 3 · Strategist

Geddes — The Analysis

The Move

Geddes doesn't test a new hypothesis. She shows that prior findings were built on sand — their research designs were flawed.

Prior FindingGeddes's Critique
"Economic crisis causes democratization"Many countries had economic crises and did not democratize. Studying only the ones that did creates a false correlation.
"Pact-making between elites leads to stable democracy"Scholars selected cases that succeeded. Cases where pacts failed (or never formed) were excluded. The finding is an artifact.
"Economic development causes democracy"When you include non-democracies, the relationship weakens considerably. Modernization theory overstates the link.
Verdict: Prior hypotheses refuted — not by finding new evidence, but by showing the old evidence was selected in a biased way.
Exemplar 3 · Strategist

Geddes — Why Refutation Is Valuable

This study's contribution is entirely negative — and that's fine.

  • Geddes doesn't propose a new theory of democratization
  • She shows that the existing evidence is unreliable
  • This is a methodological contribution — it changes how the entire field does research

Lesson for your CQ4: If your analysis doesn't support your hypothesis, don't panic. A well-designed study that honestly reports disconfirming evidence is better scholarship than a poorly designed study that "confirms" a hypothesis through selection bias. Remember KKV: "Maximise observable implications" — including the ones that might prove you wrong.

"If all your evidence confirms your theory, you probably aren't looking hard enough."
— paraphrasing KKV, Ch. 1
Exemplar 4 · Storyteller

Tannenwald — The Nuclear Taboo (1999)

The Setup

Question: Why hasn't the United States used nuclear weapons since 1945, even when it had military advantage?

Expected process: The horrified public reaction to Hiroshima/Nagasaki created a normative taboo → the taboo was internalized by policymakers → it constrained US nuclear policy during subsequent crises (Korea, Vietnam, Gulf War).

Rival pathways:

  • Deterrence: mutual assured destruction made use irrational
  • Tactical irrelevance: nukes simply weren't useful in these conflicts

Method: Process tracing through four Cold War decision points, using declassified documents, presidential memoirs, NSC minutes.

Exemplar 4 · Storyteller

Tannenwald — The Analysis

Tracing the Chain

LinkEvidenceSource Type
1. Horror reaction (1945)Public opinion data, newspaper editorials, congressional testimony documenting widespread moral revulsionDescriptive inference
2. Taboo forms (1945–53)Pattern of language in official documents shifts from "weapon of war" to "weapon of last resort"Documentary analysis
3. Korea (1950–53)Truman considered but rejected use; NSC documents show advisors explicitly cited moral/reputational costsDeclassified records
4. Vietnam (1960s)Johnson and Nixon advisors discussed nuclear options; the taboo language reappears — "unthinkable," "civilized nations"Presidential memoirs, NSC minutes

Testing the Rival (Deterrence)

If deterrence alone explained non-use, decision-makers should cite only strategic/retaliatory risks. Instead, documents show they cited moral and normative reasons — even when the adversary lacked nuclear capability (Korea, early Vietnam).

Verdict: Process fully traced. The taboo mechanism is present at every decision point, including when deterrence alone cannot explain non-use.
Exemplar 4 · Storyteller

Tannenwald — Why It Convinces

What makes this analysis exemplary:

  • Evidence at every link — not "then the taboo formed." She shows how it formed, with specific documents, specific actors, specific language.
  • Multiple decision points — tracing the same mechanism across Korea, Vietnam, and the Gulf War. Each case is an additional test.
  • The rival is traced, not just named — she shows that deterrence was present but insufficient. At points where deterrence should have mattered most (enemy has nukes), the taboo language still appears.
  • Descriptive inference matters — Collier (2011) notes that Tannenwald's first task was to establish that the horror reaction actually occurred and was widespread. This descriptive work is foundational.

Lesson for your CQ4: Name dates. Name actors. Name documents. "The process unfolded" is not evidence. "In the March 1953 NSC meeting, Eisenhower's advisor stated…" — that's evidence.

Exemplar 5 · Storyteller

Collier — Process Tracing Tests (2011)

The four tests — illustrated with Sherlock Holmes.

The Framework

Collier formalized four types of evidence tests for process tracing. Each tells you something different about your hypothesis:

TestPassingFailingAnalogy
Straw-in-the-WindSlightly strengthens hypothesisSlightly weakens itA clue that's consistent but not decisive
Hoop TestDoesn't confirm, but keeps aliveEliminates hypothesisA minimum requirement — must pass to stay
Smoking GunConfirms hypothesisDoesn't eliminate itCaught holding the weapon
Doubly DecisiveConfirms and eliminates rivalsEliminates hypothesisRare — the silver bullet
Exemplar 5 · Storyteller

Collier — The Sherlock Holmes Example

"Silver Blaze" — Who Killed the Trainer?

Three suspects: Simpson (a visitor), Straker (the trainer himself), or the horse.

EvidenceTest TypeResult
Expensive dress bill in Straker's pocket; wife doesn't knowStraw-in-the-windFinancial motive for Straker — plausible but not decisive
The dog didn't bark in the nightStraw-in-the-windIntruder was known to the dog → possibly Straker — but not confirmed
Simpson's timid appearance vs. "savage blow that shattered his head"Hoop testSimpson fails — eliminated as suspect
Straker couldn't shatter his own headHoop testStraker (self-inflicted) fails — eliminated
Maid brought curried mutton → stable boy druggedSmoking gunConfirms chain started in Straker's household
Combined weight: two suspects eliminated + multiple straws point to horseDoubly decisive (combined)Horse killed Straker ✅
Exemplar 5 · Storyteller

Collier — Why This Matters for You

You're not writing detective fiction. But the logic is identical:

  • Each piece of evidence is a test — know what kind of test it is. A straw-in-the-wind is honest. Claiming a smoking gun when you have a straw is dishonest.
  • Elimination matters as much as confirmation — showing your rival fails a hoop test is as valuable as showing your hypothesis passes a smoking gun.
  • Combined weight — four straws-in-the-wind can add up to something powerful. You don't always need a single decisive test.

Lesson for your CQ4: Label your evidence. "This constitutes a hoop test for my hypothesis" or "This is a straw-in-the-wind that is consistent with my argument." This language signals methodological awareness.

Exemplar 6 · Disruptor

Said — Orientalism (1978)

The Setup

Question: How has Western scholarly and literary discourse constructed "the Orient" as an object of knowledge and domination?

Framework: Drawing on Foucault's discourse/power nexus — Orientalism is not a collection of mistakes about the East but a system of knowledge production that makes colonial domination appear natural and inevitable.

Corpus: European literary texts, travel writing, diplomatic correspondence, and academic scholarship from the 18th–20th centuries (primarily British and French).

Method: Discourse analysis / genealogy — tracing how categories, images, and knowledge practices about "the Orient" were produced, circulated, and institutionalized.

Exemplar 6 · Disruptor

Said — The Analysis

Discursive Patterns

PatternEvidence
Binary construction"Orient" vs. "Occident" — rational/irrational, modern/backward, active/passive — recurring across hundreds of texts over two centuries
Subject positionsThe Western scholar is the knowing subject; the Oriental is the known object. This hierarchy is embedded in grammar, not just content.
Institutional productivityOrientalist discourse produced academic departments, museum collections, colonial administration categories, foreign policy doctrines
ForeclosureOriental self-representation is structurally excluded. "They cannot represent themselves; they must be represented."

The Dominant Account Challenged

The mainstream view: Western scholarship about the Middle East is objective knowledge. Said: it is a power/knowledge formation that constitutes its object in the act of studying it.

Verdict: The analysis reveals Orientalism as a constitutive discourse — not a set of facts about the East, but a Western technology for producing "the East" as a governable category.
Exemplar 6 · Disruptor

Said — Why It Convinces (and Where It's Contested)

Strengths:

  • Massive corpus — centuries of texts, not cherry-picked examples
  • Patterns, not impressions — recurring binaries across genres (literature, diplomacy, scholarship) show systemic production, not individual bias
  • Productivity shown concretely — colonial categories, academic departments, museum practices. The discourse built institutions.
  • Influenced an entire generation of postcolonial scholarship

Critiques:

  • Lewis (1982) and others: Said overgeneralizes — not all Western scholarship is Orientalist
  • Lack of systematic counting — Said's method is more interpretive than methodical
  • Agency of the "Orient" — Said focuses on Western production; voices from the East are thin

Lesson for your CQ4: A Disruptor analysis is powerful when the corpus is large, the patterns are recurring, and the productivity is concrete. It's weakened when it relies on selected quotes rather than systematic reading. Count your predicates. Name your absences.

Exemplar 7 · Disruptor

Doty — Immigration and Predicate Analysis

The Setup

Question: How does US political discourse construct immigrants as subjects?

Framework: Discourse constitutes the "immigrant" as a category — not describing pre-existing people but producing subject positions (threat, burden, victim, asset).

Method: Predicate analysis — tracking which verbs, adjectives, and metaphors are systematically attached to "immigrants" vs. "citizens" in official documents.

Corpus: Congressional hearing transcripts, INS policy documents, presidential speeches.

Exemplar 7 · Disruptor

Doty — The Analysis

Predicate Patterns

SubjectPredicates (verbs/adjectives attached)
"Illegal immigrants"flood, swarm, pour in, overrun, burden, threaten, exploit
"Citizens" / "Americans"deserve, protect, benefit, work hard, pay taxes, belong

What the Discourse Produces

A hierarchy of belonging: "citizens" as deserving subjects; "immigrants" as dehumanized flows. The hydraulic metaphor ("flood," "pour") removes agency and individuality.

What the Discourse Forecloses

The immigrant as rights-holder, contributor, or community member. These subject positions are structurally unavailable within the official discourse.

Verdict: Predicate analysis reveals a systematic discursive hierarchy. The categories are not neutral descriptions but power-laden constructions.
Exemplar 7 · Disruptor

Doty — Why Predicate Analysis Works

This is the method several of you should be using:

  • It's systematic — you identify a subject ("immigrants"), then catalogue every predicate attached to it across the corpus. This is countable. This is replicable.
  • It shows production — the predicates don't describe reality; they construct the subject. "Flood" is not a description of immigration; it's a metaphor that produces immigrants as a natural disaster.
  • Absences are data — the predicates that are never attached to "immigrants" (belong, contribute, deserve) are as important as the ones that are.

Lesson for your CQ4: If you're a Disruptor, build a predicate table. Two columns: subject and predicates. Count them. Show the pattern. Then show what's missing. That's your analysis.

What All Seven Share

Despite different tracks, methods, and verdicts.

StandardEvery Exemplar Does This
Specific evidenceNumbers, documents, predicates, dates — not "the data shows." Named, cited, verifiable.
StructureEach analysis follows a logic: present evidence → test/trace/map → address rival → state verdict.
HonestyPartial support, weak links, limitations — acknowledged, not hidden. Geddes's entire contribution is showing others were dishonest.
Rival engagementNot "I'm right" but "I'm more convincing than the alternative." The rival is tested, traced, or contrasted — not dismissed.
ReplicabilityAnother researcher could follow the same method with the same sources and check the work.

These five standards apply to every track. The language changes. The logic doesn't.

Hour 2: Applying the Patterns

30 minutes — individual work.

Exercise: Draft Your Analysis Outline

Using the exemplar(s) mapped to your project, draft a one-page analysis outline for your Core Quest 4:

  1. Evidence inventory — list every piece of evidence you have or will collect. Be specific: data source, dates, what it measures.
  2. Test/trace/map plan — for each piece of evidence, state what it tests (Strategist), which link it supports (Storyteller), or which pattern it reveals (Disruptor).
  3. Rival engagement — name the rival and identify which of your evidence addresses it.
  4. Anticipated verdict — based on what you know so far, what do you expect? Supported? Partial? Revealed?
  5. Weak spot — where is your evidence thinnest? Name it now so you can address it.

Use the Analysis Walkthrough app (tools/analysis-walkthrough.html) if you want structured prompts.

The Bottom Line

Good analysis is not about being right. It's about being honest, specific, and systematic.

Putnam tested three observable implications — and the 100-year test made the case.
Norris & Inglehart found "most consistent" support — and named what was weaker.
Geddes showed everyone else was wrong — and that was the contribution.
Tannenwald traced every link with documents — and named the specific actors.
Collier taught us to label our evidence — straw, hoop, or smoking gun.
Said mapped centuries of texts — and showed how knowledge produces power.
Doty counted predicates — and showed what was absent.
