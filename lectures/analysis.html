The Analysis Section: What Are You Actually Doing?

The analysis is where you confront your theory with your evidence. It's the most important — and most misunderstood — part of a research paper.

"A research paper is not a book report. It is an argument supported by evidence."
— Baglione, Ch. 8

The question every analysis must answer:

Does the evidence support, complicate, or contradict your argument?

How you answer that question depends on your track. The evidence is different. The standards are different. The language is different. But the logic is the same: you made a claim, now you must show whether reality cooperates.

Strategist Track

Analysis: The Strategist Way

Test, compare, rule out.

For the Strategist, analysis means testing observable implications of your hypothesis against empirical evidence. The steps:

  1. Present the evidence — data, statistics, case comparisons. Be specific: numbers, dates, sources.
  2. Test each observable implication — does what you predicted actually appear in the data? Walk through them one by one.
  3. Compare across cases — if using MSSD/MDSD, show how the independent variable varies and whether the dependent variable moves accordingly.
  4. Address the rival — test the alternative explanation with the same evidence. Does it fit better, worse, or equally well?
  5. State the verdict — hypothesis supported, partially supported, or refuted. No hedging without evidence for the hedge.
"We should always try to find observable implications that distinguish our theory from its rivals."
— KKV, Ch. 1
Strategist Track

Worked Example: Populism in Italy vs. Germany

MSSD — Economic Crisis → Populist Support

Hypothesis: Severe economic crisis increases populist party support via erosion of trust in mainstream institutions.

Rival: Cultural backlash (Norris & Inglehart 2019) — identity threat, not economic pain, drives populism.

Cases: Italy (Five Star Movement) vs. Germany (AfD) — both EU members, both hit by 2008 crisis, but different severity.

The Evidence:

IndicatorItalyGermany
GDP contraction (2008–2013)−8.5%−5.6% (recovered by 2011)
Unemployment change6.7% → 12.7%7.8% → 5.2%
Trust in parliament (Eurobarometer)29% → 11%40% → 44%
Populist vote share (peak)M5S: 32.7% (2018)AfD: 12.6% (2017)
Strategist Track

Strategist Analysis: The Verdict

Testing the Observable Implications:

  • OI-1: The country with the deeper crisis should show higher populist vote share. ✅ Italy: 32.7% vs. Germany: 12.6%.
  • OI-2: The mechanism — institutional trust — should erode more where the crisis was deeper. ✅ Italy: 18-point drop vs. Germany: 4-point increase.
  • OI-3: Timing — populist rise should follow economic distress, not precede it. ✅ M5S founded 2009, peaked 2018; AfD founded 2013 (Eurozone crisis context).

Testing the Rival:

If cultural backlash were the primary driver, Germany — with its higher immigration exposure post-2015 — should show greater populist growth. Instead, AfD peaked at 12.6% while M5S hit 32.7%. The cultural backlash thesis does not explain the Italian case well.

Verdict:

Hypothesis supported. Economic severity, mediated by trust erosion, better explains the variance in populist support between these two cases. However, the AfD's rise post-2015 suggests cultural factors may serve as a secondary amplifier — the analysis is honest about this.

Key lesson: "Supported" does not mean "proven." It means your evidence fits your theory better than the rival's — for now, in these cases, with this data.

Storyteller Track

Analysis: The Storyteller Way

Trace, narrate, reconstruct.

For the Storyteller, analysis means tracing the causal mechanism through a sequence of evidence. You're not testing a correlation — you're reconstructing how an outcome came about. The steps:

  1. Establish the starting conditions — what was the situation before your causal process began? Set the baseline.
  2. Trace each link in the chain — show evidence for each step of the mechanism: A led to B, B led to C, C led to the outcome. Each link needs its own evidence.
  3. Show the evidence at each step — documents, interviews, speeches, decisions, observable actions. Name dates, actors, and sources.
  4. Identify the breaks — where the chain didn't work as expected. Where did the process diverge, stall, or produce something unexpected?
  5. Assess the alternative pathway — could a different mechanism have produced the same outcome? Trace it and show where the evidence favors your pathway over the rival's.
"Process tracing attempts to identify the intervening causal process — the causal chain and causal mechanism — between an independent variable and the outcome of the dependent variable."
— George & Bennett, Ch. 6
Storyteller Track

Worked Example: Youth Trust in the Turkish Parliament

Process Tracing — Institutional Change → Declining Trust

Question: Why has trust in the Turkish parliament among young people dropped to 14%?

Expected process: Institutional centralization → reduced parliamentary autonomy → perceived irrelevance among youth → trust decline.

Rival pathway: Social media exposure → generalized cynicism → trust decline (not institution-specific).

Tracing the Mechanism:

LinkEvidenceSource
1. Institutional centralization (2017 referendum)Constitutional shift to presidential system; parliament loses legislative initiativeOfficial Gazette, constitutional text
2. Reduced parliamentary autonomyLegislative output drops 34% (2016–2020); 78% of laws initiated by executiveTBMM records
3. Youth perception of irrelevanceSurvey data: 78% of respondents endorse democracy but only 14% trust parliament specificallyWorld Values Survey, Eurobarometer Turkey
4. Trust declineTrust in parliament falls from 41% (2013) to 14% (2023) among 18–25 cohortWVS longitudinal data
Storyteller Track

Storyteller Analysis: The Verdict

Testing the Rival Pathway:

If generalized social media cynicism were the driver, we'd expect trust in all institutions to decline equally among youth — not parliament specifically. But the data shows: trust in the military (48%) and the judiciary (31%) declined less steeply. The distrust is targeted at parliament. This is inconsistent with a generalized cynicism explanation.

The Break:

Link 2→3 is the weakest: the analysis shows parliament lost autonomy and youth distrust parliament, but the causal step — that youth noticed and responded to reduced autonomy — is inferred, not directly observed. An interview or focus-group study would strengthen this link.

Verdict:

Expected process partially traced. Three of four links are supported by direct evidence. The mechanism holds, but the perceptual link (how youth became aware of institutional change) requires further evidence.

Key lesson: A Storyteller doesn't say "hypothesis confirmed." A Storyteller says "the chain holds at these links, weakens at this link, and the rival pathway cannot account for the targeted nature of the distrust." Honesty about weak links is a strength, not a failure.

Disruptor Track

Analysis: The Disruptor Way

Read, map, excavate, expose.

For the Disruptor, analysis means showing what discourse produces and forecloses. You're not testing a hypothesis — you're excavating a system of meaning. The steps:

  1. Describe the corpus — what texts, how many, from when, produced by whom, and why these. Be precise about boundaries.
  2. Map the discursive patterns — recurring terms, metaphors, categories, binaries. Use frequency, collocation, or predicate analysis to show patterns systematically (not anecdotally).
  3. Show what the discourse produces — what subjects, objects, categories, or policies does this language make possible? What becomes "natural" or "obvious"?
  4. Show what the discourse forecloses — what is absent, silenced, or made unthinkable? What alternatives cannot be articulated within this framework?
  5. Contrast with the dominant account — how does the mainstream reading of this issue differ from what your analysis reveals?
"Discourse studies focus on how discourses produce the subjects authorized to speak and act, how they construct the knowledgeable practices that become common sense, and what they exclude as impractical or unthinkable."
— Milliken (1999), p. 229
Disruptor Track

Worked Example: EU Border Policy as a "Caste System"

CDA — Sanctions & Border Regimes as Tools of Exclusion

Question: How does EU border policy discourse construct mobility as a right for some and a threat when exercised by others?

Framework: Border discourse produces a hierarchy of movement — "mobility" for EU citizens, "migration" for non-EU subjects — that functions as a system of racialized exclusion.

Dominant account: EU border policy is a security measure designed to manage flows and protect citizens.

The Corpus:

35 EU policy documents (2015–2023): European Agenda on Migration, Frontex annual risk analyses, European Council conclusions on migration. Selected because they represent official EU discourse — the language that shapes policy, funding, and enforcement.

Disruptor Track

Disruptor Analysis: Mapping the Discourse

Discursive Patterns (Predicate Analysis):

TermFrequency (35 docs)Typical Predicates
"irregular migration"247 mentionsmanaged, combated, prevented, reduced
"labour mobility"83 mentionsfacilitated, encouraged, protected, promoted
"flows"189 mentionsstemmed, diverted, controlled (hydraulic metaphor)
"people" / "persons"41 mentionsvulnerable, rescued, returned, relocated

What the Discourse Produces:

A two-tier system of movement: "mobility" (positive, European, facilitated) vs. "migration" (threatening, non-European, controlled). The hydraulic metaphor ("flows," "stemmed," "diverted") dehumanizes — people become a liquid to be managed, not individuals with agency.

What the Discourse Forecloses:

The language makes it nearly impossible to articulate a right to move for non-EU subjects. "Mobility" is never used for African or Middle Eastern movement. The framework excludes the possibility that borders themselves — not "irregular" movement — are the problem.

Disruptor Track

Disruptor Analysis: The Verdict

Contrasting the Dominant Account:

The official framing presents border policy as neutral security management. The analysis reveals it as a constitutive practice — the language doesn't just describe migration, it creates the categories through which some bodies are mobile and others are threats. The 6:1 ratio of "irregular migration" to "people" tells us what the discourse considers worth naming.

Verdict:

The analysis reveals a discursive hierarchy that mirrors colonial-era distinctions between civilized mobility and uncivilized movement. This is not proven in the Strategist sense — it is shown through systematic textual evidence. The Disruptor doesn't "confirm a hypothesis." The Disruptor makes visible what the dominant account renders invisible.

Key lesson: A Disruptor's analysis is rigorous not because it's falsifiable, but because it's systematic — frequencies, patterns, predicates, absences. Impressionistic readings ("I feel like the language is biased") are not analysis. Counted predicates across a defined corpus are.

From Analysis to Conclusion

Baglione, Ch. 10

The conclusion is not a summary. It answers four questions:

1. So what?

Why do your findings matter — for the scholarly debate, for policy, for the real world?

2. What did you find?

State the main finding in 2–3 sentences. Not "I looked at X" — rather "I found that X, which means Y."

3. What are the limitations?

Every study has limits. Name yours. Limited cases, limited time, missing data, weak links. This is a strength, not a confession.

4. What comes next?

If someone were to continue this research, what should they do? More cases? Different method? Longer time frame? New corpus?

Your conclusion should be 300–500 words. Short, direct, honest.

Hour 2: The Abstract

The hardest 150 words you'll ever write.

An abstract is not a summary. It is a compressed argument. It tells the reader: what you asked, why it matters, how you investigated, what you found, and what it means — in 150–200 words.

Why now? Two reasons:

  • The Research Symposium (Week 15) requires one — it's your "conference submission."
  • Writing the abstract forces clarity. If you can't compress your design into 150 words, you don't fully understand it yet.
"If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough."
— attributed to Einstein (but good advice regardless)

The Five-Sentence Abstract

A formula to get you started (then break it later).

SentenceFunctionStarts with…
1The puzzle — why should anyone care?"Despite…" / "While scholars agree…" / "The rise of…"
2The gap — what the literature misses"Existing accounts overlook…" / "Less understood is…"
3The argument — your theory in one sentence"This paper argues that…" / "I contend that…"
4The method — how you test/trace/analyze"Using MSSD / process tracing / CDA of…"
5The contribution — what this adds"This study contributes to… by showing…"

Five sentences. ~150 words. This is your elevator pitch, your conference proposal, and your North Star for writing the paper.

Strategist Example

Sample Abstract: Populism in Crisis

(1) The 2008 financial crisis reshaped European party systems, but populist parties surged in some countries and stalled in others. (2) Most comparative studies focus on demand-side cultural explanations, overlooking how institutional trust collapse mediates the economic-to-political transmission. (3) This paper argues that the severity of economic crisis drives populist support through the erosion of trust in mainstream parliamentary institutions. (4) Using a most-similar systems design comparing Italy's Five Star Movement and Germany's Alternative for Germany, the study tests three observable implications drawn from institutional trust theory against Eurobarometer and electoral data (2008–2018). (5) The findings contribute to the comparative populism literature by demonstrating that economic severity, not cultural backlash alone, explains divergent populist trajectories within the EU's core.

Storyteller Example

Sample Abstract: Youth & Parliament

(1) Despite endorsing democratic values at high rates, Turkish youth report among the lowest levels of trust in parliament in Europe — just 14%. (2) Current explanations attribute this to generalized political cynicism or social media exposure, but neither accounts for why distrust is targeted at parliament specifically. (3) This paper argues that the 2017 constitutional shift to a presidential system reduced parliamentary autonomy, rendering the institution irrelevant in the eyes of politically aware youth. (4) Through process tracing of the institutional-change-to-perception pathway — drawing on legislative output data, constitutional texts, and World Values Survey longitudinal panels — the study reconstructs how institutional centralization translated into targeted distrust among 18–25-year-olds. (5) The analysis contributes to democratization studies by showing how formal institutional redesign can erode the legitimacy of representative bodies even among citizens who remain committed to democracy in principle.

Disruptor Example

Sample Abstract: Borders & Mobility

(1) The European Union frames its border regime as a neutral instrument of security management, yet movement within and toward Europe is governed by starkly unequal logics. (2) Critical migration scholarship has examined policy outcomes, but less attention has been paid to how official EU discourse constitutes the categories through which some movement is celebrated as "mobility" and other movement is policed as "migration." (3) This paper argues that EU border discourse produces a racialized hierarchy of movement that mirrors colonial-era distinctions between civilized and uncivilized subjects. (4) Through critical discourse analysis of 35 EU policy documents (2015–2023) — including the European Agenda on Migration, Frontex risk analyses, and European Council conclusions — the study maps predicate patterns, metaphor systems, and strategic silences. (5) The analysis contributes to critical border studies by making visible the discursive architecture through which Europe's right to exclude is naturalized as governance rather than recognized as power.

Live Drafting: Write Your Abstract Now

20 minutes. Five sentences. 150 words.

Instructions:

  1. Open a blank document or your notes app.
  2. Write one sentence per line, following the five-sentence formula.
  3. Don't overthink the first draft — you will revise it.
  4. Aim for 150 words. If you're at 200+, cut adjectives and qualifiers first.

The Formula:

Sentence 1: The puzzle — what's surprising, important, or unexplained?
Sentence 2: The gap — what does existing scholarship miss?
Sentence 3: The argument — what do you claim?
Sentence 4: The method — how do you investigate?
Sentence 5: The contribution — what does this add to knowledge?

After 20 minutes, swap with a neighbor. Read theirs. Can you identify all five elements? If not, that's the feedback.

Peer Swap: 10 Minutes

Read your partner's abstract. Answer three questions.

  1. Can you identify all five sentences? (puzzle, gap, argument, method, contribution) — If any is missing, circle where it should go.
  2. After reading this, do you know what the paper is about? — If not, what's unclear?
  3. Is there an argument, or just a topic? — "This paper examines…" is a topic. "This paper argues…" is an argument.

Return the abstract with your notes. The author revises tonight and brings a clean version to the Symposium.

The Pivot Badge (+2 Bonus)

The Pivot Badge rewards students who make a significant, documented revision to their research design based on feedback from the Saboteurs Workshop or the Research Panels.

To earn the badge:

  1. In your Core Quest 3 (or Core Quest 4), include a footnote or paragraph that explains: "Based on feedback from [name the source], I revised [specific element] from [old version] to [new version] because [reason]."
  2. The revision must be substantive — not fixing typos, but changing your cases, redefining your variable, adding a rival, switching methods, or reframing your question.

Examples of qualifying pivots:

  • Switched from 1 case to 2 after the Saboteur identified selection bias
  • Added a rival explanation based on a panelist's suggestion
  • Reframed the research question from descriptive ("what") to explanatory ("why")
  • Changed method from large-N to MSSD after realizing N was insufficient

+2 bonus points. Verified by the instructor when grading CQ3 or CQ4.

Week 15: The Research Symposium

June 1–3 · Your Academic Conference

The Research Symposium is your chance to present your work the way scholars do: standing before peers, defending your argument, fielding questions.

Format:

  • 5-minute presentation per student — slides optional but recommended (3–5 slides max)
  • 3-minute Q&A — questions from classmates and the instructor
  • Panels of 3–4 students grouped by theme (same or revised from Week 12 panels)

What you present:

  • Research question & why it matters (30 seconds)
  • Theory & argument — your causal claim or analytical framework (1 minute)
  • Method, cases, and data (1 minute)
  • Preliminary findings or expected findings (1.5 minutes)
  • One honest limitation and next steps (1 minute)
