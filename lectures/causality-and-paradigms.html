The Core Idea
Saying "X causes Y" is not enough.
Anyone can claim a correlation. A research design requires you to explain how X produces Y — the chain of steps, the mechanism, the engine that makes it work.

The Problem
Not everyone agrees on what "causation" means.
Your paradigm track doesn't just change your method. It changes what counts as an explanation, what counts as evidence, and what the causal engine even looks like.
Three Engines, Three Logics
How each track thinks about causation

The Strategist

Causal logic: X causes Y. Find the independent variable, measure it, test it. Causation is regularity — if X, then Y, holding all else constant.

Engine type: Hypothesis → Variables → Test

"Regime type (X) affects treaty compliance (Y) because democratic institutions raise the cost of defection."

The Storyteller

Causal logic: X constitutes or shapes Y through meaning. Causation is process — trace how actors interpret situations and how those interpretations produce outcomes.

Engine type: Context → Interpretation → Action

"The 'nuclear taboo' shapes state behavior not through material cost-benefit but through normative constraint — leaders don't use nukes because they've internalized it as unthinkable."

The Disruptor

Causal logic: Power structures produce and reproduce conditions. Causation is systemic — trace how discourse, institutions, and hierarchies create what appears "natural."

Engine type: Power → Discourse → Reality

"European migration policy doesn't respond to a security 'threat' — it constructs African migrants as a threat through securitizing discourse, justifying exclusion."
The Strategist's Engine
If X, then Y — and here's why.
The positivist causal engine has three parts: an independent variable (the cause), a dependent variable (the outcome), and a mechanism that explains how one produces the other. Your job is to isolate this mechanism and test whether it holds.

The Strategist's Question

Can I observe variation in X and corresponding variation in Y? Can I rule out alternative explanations? Is the mechanism testable?

The Storyteller's Engine
How do actors make sense of their world — and how does that produce outcomes?
The interpretivist engine doesn't isolate variables — it traces processes. How did actors interpret a situation? How did those meanings shape what they did? The mechanism is meaning-making itself.

The critical engine doesn't just explain outcomes — it questions the categories. Why is this called a "crisis"? Who defined "security"? The mechanism is power operating through discourse, institutions, and knowledge production.

X: Treaty ratification
Mechanism: Domestic groups use treaty commitments to mobilize and litigate

Storyteller

Context: Norm diffusion through IOs
Mechanism: States internalize human rights norms through socialization, not just cost-benefit
Outcome: Compliance as identity change

Finnemore (1996) — trace how IOs taught states to value human rights

Power structure: Western-defined rights framework
Mechanism: "Compliance" is measured by Western standards, silencing alternative conceptions of rights
Outcome: Non-compliance as resistance, not failure

Your Turn
Build Your Causal Engine
1

State your research question

What are you trying to explain? Write it as a clear "why" or "how" question.

2

Identify X and Y

What is the cause (or condition, or discourse) and what is the outcome (or practice, or construction)?

3

Name the mechanism

What happens in between? How does X produce Y? This is the hardest part — and the most important.

4

Test it against a rival

What would someone from a different track say? What's the alternative engine? If you can't state the rival, you don't understand the debate yet.

Warning
Fatal Flaws in Causal Reasoning

Correlation ≠ Causation

"Countries with more ice cream sales have higher crime rates." Both are caused by summer heat — not each other.

Fix: Name the mechanism. If you can't explain HOW, you haven't proven causation.

Reverse Causality

"Democracy causes economic growth." Or does economic growth create conditions for democracy?

Fix: Establish temporal sequence. Which came first? Use process tracing.

Omitted Variable

You found X → Y, but actually Z causes both. You left Z out of your model.

Fix: Consider alternative explanations explicitly. What else could explain Y?

Tautology

"Powerful states are powerful because they have power." Your explanation restates the thing you're trying to explain.

Fix: Make sure X and Y are defined independently of each other.
More Fatal Flaws
These Will Sink Your Research Design

Unfalsifiability

"Culture explains everything." If your theory can explain any outcome, it explains nothing. A good theory must be able to be wrong.

Fix: Ask yourself — what evidence would prove my argument wrong? If nothing could, redesign.

Selection Bias

You only study cases where Y happened. You never look at cases where X was present but Y didn't occur.

Fix: Include variation in your dependent variable. Study both successes and failures.

Concept Stretching

Using "populism" to describe everything from Trump to Erdoğan to Chávez without defining it precisely.

Fix: Define your key concepts clearly. What counts and what doesn't? Draw boundaries.

Normative Masquerading as Empirical

"States should comply with human rights treaties" disguised as "States comply with treaties when..." Mixing ought with is.

Fix: Be explicit about whether your claim is normative or empirical. Both are valid — but don't confuse them.
The Bridge
Your Literature Map IS Your Debate
Between Causal Engines
Look at your literature map. Each school of thought isn't just a different "perspective" — it's a different causal engine. The positivist sources offer one mechanism. The interpretivist sources offer another. The critical sources question the entire framing.
Your Literature Review tells the story of these competing engines. Your Research Design commits to one — and explains why it's the most convincing one for your puzzle.

The Key Move

A research design is not "I think X causes Y." It is: "Here is my causal engine, here is how I will test it, and here is what would convince me I'm wrong."
