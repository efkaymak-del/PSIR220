A research design is not a summary of your topic. It's not "what I'm interested in." It's the plan — the logic that connects your question to your evidence to your answer.

Today: what that plan looks like, and what the best scholars in the field say it must contain.

Baglione's Two Analogies

Baglione (Ch. 7) says a research design is like two things at once:

The Courtroom

You are a lawyer making a case before a skeptical jury. Your research design is your argument: here is my claim, here is my evidence, here is why you should believe me and not the opposing counsel. Every element must hold up to cross-examination.

If the jury isn't convinced, it doesn't matter how passionate you are.

Medical Science

You are a doctor diagnosing a patient. The symptoms are the data. The diagnosis is your theory. The treatment plan is your method. But you must also consider differential diagnoses — what else could explain these symptoms? If you skip that step, you might kill the patient.

Alternative explanations are not optional.

Both analogies make the same point: a research design is an argument for why your approach will produce a valid answer.

Strategist Track

KKV: The Four Components

King, Keohane, and Verba argue that all research designs — quantitative or qualitative — share the same logic of inference. Their four components:

"We divide all research designs into four components: the research question, the theory, the data, and the use of the data."

— KKV, Designing Social Inquiry, Ch. 1 (p. 12–13)

The research question must be "important in the real world" and "make a specific contribution to an identifiable scholarly literature." Not just interesting — consequential and positioned.

The theory must generate observable implications — predictions that can be checked against evidence. The more observable implications, the stronger the design.

Strategist Track

Observable Implications: The KKV Test

"Whether we study many phenomena or few — or even one — the study will be improved if we collect data on as many observable implications of our theory as possible."

— KKV, Designing Social Inquiry, Ch. 1 (p. 12)

This is the central Strategist move: your theory predicts things. A good research design identifies those predictions and tests them. The more tests you can run, the more confident you can be.

Example: If your theory is that "economic crisis causes populist voting," the observable implications include: populist parties should gain votes after recessions; regions hit hardest should show the biggest gains; countries with strong safety nets should show smaller effects; the pattern should hold across multiple elections.

Each implication is a test. Each test that passes strengthens the design. Each test that fails forces revision.

Strategist Track

KKV's Rules for Causal Theories

KKV Chapter 3 lays out five rules for constructing causal theories. If you're a Strategist, these are your commandments:

#RuleWhat It Means
1Construct falsifiable theoriesState what evidence would disprove your argument. If nothing could, it's not a theory.
2Internal consistencyYour assumptions can't contradict each other. If they do, any conclusion follows.
3Select dependent variables carefullyDon't study only cases where the outcome happened. Include variation.
4Maximize concretenessVague theories are untestable. Define your variables precisely.
5State theories as encompassingly as feasibleDon't limit your theory to one case if it could apply to many. Broader scope = more tests.

Notice: these are all about protecting yourself from criticism — exactly Baglione's framing.

Storyteller Track

George & Bennett: Structured, Focused Comparison

George and Bennett offer the Storyteller's answer to research design. Their key method — structured, focused comparison — has two requirements:

"The method is 'structured' in that the researcher writes general questions that reflect the research objective and that these questions are asked of each case under study to guide and standardize data collection. The method is 'focused' in that it deals only with certain aspects of the historical cases examined."

— George & Bennett, Case Studies and Theory Development, Ch. 3 (p. 67)

This matters because earlier case study research was criticized for being "noncumulative" — each study went its own way, reflecting the researcher's interests rather than a systematic design. Structured, focused comparison solved this.

Storyteller Track

George & Bennett: Five Design Tasks

Their Chapter 4 outlines five tasks for designing case study research — the Storyteller's equivalent of KKV's four components:

TaskWhat You DoWhy It Matters
1. Specify the problemDefine the class of events your case belongs toYour case must be an instance of something — not just "interesting"
2. Specify variablesIdentify DV, IV, and intervening variablesEven qualitative work requires clear concepts
3. Select casesChoose cases relevant to the research objective"Cases should not be selected simply because they are interesting, important, or easily researched"
4. Describe varianceHow do your variables change across cases?Without variance, there's nothing to explain
5. Formulate expectationsWhat do you expect to find, and why?This is your theoretical argument

"The primary criterion for case selection should be relevance to the research objective of the study, whether it includes theory development, theory testing, or heuristic purposes."

— George & Bennett, Ch. 4 (p. 83)
Storyteller Track

Disruptor Track

The Disruptor's Research Design

KKV and George & Bennett write primarily for Strategists and Storytellers. What about Disruptors? The critical tradition doesn't abandon design — it redefines it.

Jennifer Milliken identifies three commitments that structure discourse analysis as a research design:

Discourse studies in IR share three theoretical commitments: discourses as systems of signification that construct social realities; a focus on discursive productivity — what discourses make possible and what they shut down; and a commitment to studying how dominant meanings are produced and challenged.

— Milliken, "The Study of Discourse in International Relations" (1999), p. 225–229

The Disruptor's design still needs structure: a defined corpus of texts, a systematic method of analysis, a clear articulation of what the discourse produces, and an account of what it excludes.

Disruptor Track

Designing a Discourse Analysis

A Disruptor research design must answer these questions:

ComponentStrategist EquivalentWhat You Specify
DiscourseIndependent variableWhat system of meaning are you analyzing? (e.g., EU migration policy documents)
ProductivityDependent variableWhat does this discourse make possible or foreclose? (e.g., construction of migrants as threats)
CorpusCase selection / dataWhich texts, speeches, or documents? What time period? Why these and not others?
Analytical methodMethodHow will you read the texts? (predicate analysis, genealogy, narrative analysis)
Positionality—Where do you stand in relation to this discourse? The Disruptor names their position explicitly.

The design is different, but the logic is the same: justify every choice, anticipate every criticism.

Three Designs, One Logic

Despite their differences, all three tracks demand the same thing: a coherent plan where each element justifies the next.

Strategist

Question: Why does X cause Y?

Theory: Hypothesis with observable implications

Method: Large-N, QCA, MSSD/MDSD

Cases: Selected for variation in X and Y

Key test: Can I falsify this?

KKV: "Maximize observable implications"

Storyteller

Question: How did this outcome come about?

Theory: Causal mechanism traced through process

Method: Process tracing, structured comparison

Cases: Selected for theoretical relevance

Key test: Can I trace the mechanism?

G&B: "Structured, focused, cumulative"

Disruptor

Question: How does power produce this reality?

Theory: Discourse as constitutive of practice

Method: Discourse analysis, genealogy

Cases: Defined corpus with justification

Key test: Can I show what's excluded?

Milliken: "Discursive productivity"

Baglione: Writing the Research Design

Baglione Ch. 7 gives you the practical structure. Your written research design must contain:

SectionContentFunction
IntroductionYour research question + why it mattersThe courtroom opening statement
Literature positionWhere you stand in the scholarly debateShows you know the field and have found a gap
Theory / argumentYour causal claim: X → M → YThe engine — what you built in Week 6
Hypothesis or expectationsWhat you expect to find if your theory is rightObservable implications (Strategist) or expected process (Storyteller)
Research design properMethod + cases + data sources + justificationThe plan of attack — how you'll generate evidence
Alternative explanationsWhat else could explain Y? How will you rule it out?The differential diagnosis — Baglione's medical analogy

If you skip alternative explanations, you're a doctor who prescribes medicine without checking whether the patient has a different disease. This is where most undergrad designs fail.

The Rival Explanation

This is the single most important thing the Saboteurs should have tested — and the single most common omission in your designs.

"Social science research at its best is a creative process of insight and discovery taking place within a well-established structure of scientific inquiry. The first-rate social scientist does not regard a research design as a blueprint for a mechanical process of data-gathering and evaluation."

— KKV, Designing Social Inquiry, Ch. 1 (p. 12)

The creative part is imagining what could prove you wrong. A design without a rival explanation is not a design — it's an assertion.

Bad: No Rival

"Economic crisis causes populism." (What if it's cultural backlash? Immigration anxiety? Institutional failure?)

Good: Named Rival

"I argue economic crisis drives populism via left-behind voters, but the cultural backlash thesis (Norris & Inglehart 2019) offers a competing mechanism. My case selection is designed to distinguish between them."

Selection Bias: The Silent Killer

KKV devote an entire chapter (Ch. 4) to what you choose to observe — and what happens when you choose wrong.

"Selection bias occurs when observations are chosen in a way that is correlated with the dependent variable... This can produce dramatically misleading inferences."

— KKV, Ch. 4

The classic mistake: You want to study why revolutions succeed. So you study five successful revolutions. You find they all had economic crises beforehand. Conclusion: economic crisis causes revolution. Wrong.

You never looked at countries that had economic crises but didn't have revolutions. That's selecting on the dependent variable.

George & Bennett's fix: select cases for theoretical relevance, not for interesting outcomes. Use most-similar, most-different, deviant, or crucial case logic — and justify it in writing.

The Research Design Is the Argument

Here's what students get wrong: they think the research design is a bureaucratic requirement — fill out the form, check the boxes, submit.

It's not. The research design is the intellectual work. It's the argument for why your approach will produce knowledge that we can trust. Everything else — data collection, analysis, writing — follows from it.

"If a research design proves inadequate, it will be difficult to achieve the research objectives of the study."

— George & Bennett, Ch. 4 (p. 73–74)

Baglione's lawyer analogy: you wouldn't walk into court and say "I feel like my client is innocent." You'd present evidence, anticipate the prosecution's counter-arguments, and explain why your evidence is more convincing. That's a research design.

Fatal Flaws in Research Designs

From Baglione, KKV, and George & Bennett — the things that sink a design:

Design Killers

  • No rival explanation — you haven't considered alternatives
  • Selecting on the DV — only studying cases where Y happened
  • Tautological mechanism — X causes Y because of X
  • Unfalsifiable theory — nothing could prove you wrong
  • Method-paradigm mismatch — discourse analysis for a Strategist question

Fixable Weaknesses

  • Question too broad — narrow the scope, add conditions
  • Vague mechanism — specify the steps, name the process
  • Convenience cases — articulate the selection logic
  • Missing sources — check access, find alternatives
  • Weak lit position — identify the gap more precisely

The red column requires a redesign. The amber column requires revision. Your Saboteur Report tells you which column you're in.

The Bottom Line

A research design is not a form to fill out.
It is not a topic statement.
It is not "what I'm interested in."

It is the argument for why your plan will produce knowledge we can trust.

Baglione's lawyer builds a case. KKV's scientist maximizes observable implications. George & Bennett's comparativist structures and focuses. Milliken's critic names what the discourse produces and excludes.

Different languages. Same demand: justify every choice. Anticipate every criticism. Name every rival.
