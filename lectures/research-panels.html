The Saboteurs asked: is this design internally sound? Today's question is different: how does this design fit into a larger scholarly conversation — and how can we make it stronger together?

Why Panels?

In academic conferences, papers aren't presented in isolation. They're grouped into panels — clusters of 3–4 papers that share a theme, a method, or a puzzle.

The panel format forces you to think across projects:

  • What connects these papers? What's the common thread?
  • What does each paper contribute that the others can't?
  • If these three papers were published in the same journal issue, what would the introduction say?

This is the skill that separates someone who can do research from someone who can think about research. You need both.

Academic research can sometimes feel like a collection of cottage industries — everyone working in isolation on their own narrow problem. Panels are how we connect those industries into a field.

Round 1: Finding the Thread

15 minutes. Before you present individual designs, step back and look at the panel as a whole.

You've all read each other's Design Briefs. Now discuss:

  • What connects these three projects? Is it a shared region, a similar puzzle, a common method, the same paradigm — or something less obvious?
  • Where do they diverge? What does each design do that the others can't or don't?
  • If you were writing the introduction to a journal special issue containing all three papers, what would it say?

This isn't a warm-up exercise. The thread you identify here should inform the feedback you give in Rounds 2 and 3. When you know how the designs relate, you can see what each one needs to do to position itself in the conversation.

Round 2: Presentations & Feedback

30 minutes total — 10 minutes per person.

The presenter (5 min): Walk your panel through your design. Don't read the brief — talk through it. Cover:

  • Your research question and why it matters
  • Your causal argument (X → M → Y) or analytical framework
  • Your method, cases, and key sources
  • Your biggest unresolved issue — be honest about what's not working yet

The panel (5 min): Respond constructively. Not "this is wrong" but "this would be stronger if..." Focus on:

  • Is the question clearly positioned in the literature?
  • Does the method match the question and the track?
  • What would make this more convincing to a skeptical reader?
  • Do you know a source, a case, or a framing that could help?

Round 3: Cross-Pollination

20 minutes. This is the most important round.

Now that you've heard all three designs and discussed the thread, ask yourselves:

  • Can I offer a source? "I read something for my annotated bibliography that's directly relevant to your argument — here's the citation."
  • Can I offer a case? "You're studying democratic backsliding in Hungary — have you considered comparing it to Poland? I've been reading about both."
  • Can I offer a reframing? "Your question might be sharper if you framed it as a 'how' question instead of a 'why' question — here's why..."
  • Can I offer a rival? "From my track, I'd see this puzzle differently — here's the alternative mechanism, and dealing with it would strengthen your design."

This is where the Methodological Savior (+2 bonus) happens. If someone's feedback demonstrably improves your Core Quest 3, you can nominate them.

Constructive vs. Critical

The Saboteurs Workshop rewarded you for finding flaws. Today rewards you for making someone's work better. Here's the difference:

Critical (Saboteur mode)

"Your causal mechanism is circular."

"You selected on the dependent variable."

"This method doesn't fit your track."

Identifies what's wrong.

Constructive (Panel mode)

"Your mechanism would be stronger if you specified the step between X and M — maybe institutional capacity is the missing link."

"Adding a negative case (where X was present but Y didn't happen) would make your case selection more convincing."

"Have you looked at Finnemore (1996)? Her norm diffusion argument might help you frame the Storyteller version of your theory."

Shows how to fix it — and offers specific resources.

How I Grouped You

Some of you will look at your panel and think: "these projects have nothing in common." Look harder. Here are the types of threads I used:

Thread TypeExampleWhat to Discuss
ThematicThree papers on conflict — Cyprus, Kurdish question, SyriaHow does each design approach violence differently? What can they learn from each other's cases?
MethodologicalThree process-tracing studies on different topicsHow does each define "the mechanism"? Are the standards of evidence the same?
Cross-paradigmOne Strategist, one Storyteller, one Disruptor — same region or themeWhat does each paradigm reveal that the others miss? Where do they contradict each other?
Puzzle matchTwo similar questions, different methodsWhy did you choose different approaches to the same puzzle? What are the trade-offs?

The point is not that your projects are the same — it's that they speak to each other in ways that aren't obvious until you read them side by side.
