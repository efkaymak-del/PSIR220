The Logic
Peer review is not about reassurance.
It's about survival.
Every published article survives hostile reviewers. Every successful dissertation survives its committee. Every conference paper survives the Q&A. If your design can't survive a classmate with a worksheet, it won't survive the real world.
Two Kinds of Peer Review
Last week vs. this week

Week 10 — Pre-Flight Check

Supportive. Your partner helped you find problems. The tone was collaborative: "Have you thought about...?" The goal was to identify issues together.

Week 11 — Saboteurs Workshop

Adversarial. Your partner's job is to break your design. The tone is prosecutorial: "This doesn't work because..." The goal is to find the fatal flaw.

Both are essential. Support without challenge produces complacency. Challenge without support produces anxiety. You need both — in that order.
Rules of Engagement
How to be a good Saboteur
1

Attack the design, never the person

"Your mechanism is tautological" — yes. "You clearly didn't think about this" — no.

2

Every attack must name a specific flaw type

Use the Flaw Menu on your worksheet. "I don't like it" is not an attack. "This is selection bias because you only study cases where Y occurred" is.

3

The designer gets a rebuttal

After each attack, the designer can defend, concede, or propose a fix. Record the outcome honestly.

4

If you can't find a flaw, say so — and explain why

A clean bill of health is valuable feedback too. But you have to earn it by actually looking.

Your Arsenal
The Flaw Menu

Alignment Flaws

Track ≠ Question · Track ≠ Sources · Track ≠ Method · Method ≠ N-Size · Pathway ≠ Track

Question Flaws

Yes/No Question · Too Broad · No Puzzle · Normative Disguised as Empirical

Causal Flaws

Tautology · Missing Mechanism · Reverse Causality · Omitted Variable

Method / Case Flaws

Selection Bias · No DV Variation · Convenience Cases · Unfalsifiable · Feasibility

Every attack must reference one of these categories. No vague complaints.
Live Demo
Watch Me Sabotage a Design

Target Design

RQ: "Why do some countries accept more refugees than others?"
Track: Strategist
Pathway: Economic capacity (X) → government willingness (M) → refugee intake (Y)
Method: Process tracing, N = 1 (Germany)
Cases: Germany 2015–2020

Can you spot the problems? I see at least three...
Three Attacks
How a Saboteur would strike
1

Method ≠ N-Size (Alignment Flaw)

The question asks "why do some countries" — plural, comparative — but the method is process tracing on a single case. One case can't explain cross-national variation. Either change the question to "how did Germany decide..." or switch to MSSD with N = 2–3.

2

Selection Bias (Method / Case Flaw)

Germany is the highest-intake case in Europe. Studying only the most extreme case tells you nothing about why other countries with similar economic capacity (France, UK) took far fewer. You're selecting on the DV.

3

Missing Mechanism (Causal Flaw)

"Government willingness" is not a mechanism — it restates the outcome. Why was the German government willing? Merkel's domestic political calculation? Historical guilt? EU pressure? The mechanism needs to explain the willingness, not just assert it.

Designer's Response
Defend, Concede, or Fix
1

Attack 1 — Concede & Fix

"You're right — I'll narrow the question to 'How did Germany's domestic political context shape its decision to open borders in 2015?' and keep process tracing, N = 1."

2

Attack 2 — Defend

"With the revised question, I'm no longer explaining cross-national variation — I'm tracing a single decision. Germany is a crucial case, not a convenience case. Selection bias doesn't apply to single-case process tracing the same way."

3

Attack 3 — Concede & Fix

"Fair. I'll replace 'government willingness' with a specific mechanism: Merkel's party calculated that humanitarian framing would neutralise the AfD's early populist challenge — a domestic political strategy, not altruism."

Verdict: DAMAGED — fixable. Two concessions, one successful defence. The core survives with revisions.
Workshop Protocol
How the Next 60 Minutes Work
1

Pair up & swap Design Summaries (2 min)

Different partner from last week. Exchange your Alignment Checker output or worksheet tear-off.

2

Read & plan your attacks in silence (10 min)

Fill in Part A of the Saboteur worksheet. Identify your three strongest attacks using the Flaw Menu.

3

Round 1 — Saboteur A attacks Designer B (15 min)

Present your three attacks. The designer responds to each. Record the outcomes in Part B.

4

Round 2 — Switch roles (15 min)

Saboteur B attacks Designer A. Same protocol.

5

Verdict & Repair Plan (10 min)

Each Saboteur delivers the verdict: Survived, Damaged, or Destroyed. Designers fill in Part D — what they'll fix.

6

Class debrief (10 min)

Volunteers share the most interesting attack. We'll identify patterns across designs.
