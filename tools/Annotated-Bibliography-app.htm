Example A · Positivist — Populism in Italy vs Germany
CITATION:
Inglehart, R., & Norris, P. (2016). Trump, Brexit, and the rise of populism: Economic have-nots and cultural backlash. Harvard Kennedy School Faculty Research Working Paper Series.

WHAT IT SAYS:
Inglehart and Norris argue that the rise of populism in Western democracies is driven by two reinforcing factors: economic insecurity following the 2008 crisis and a cultural backlash among older, less educated voters against progressive social change. They draw on survey data from over 30 countries to test both explanations, finding that cultural backlash has stronger explanatory power than economic grievance alone.

MY EVALUATION:
This is a highly cited, rigorous comparative study with strong data. The limitation for my question is that it treats Western democracies as a single category — it does not explain why Italy and Germany diverged so sharply. I will use it as a theoretical baseline but need country-specific sources to explain the variation.

HOW IT CONNECTS:
This source gives me my two main independent variables — economic insecurity and cultural backlash — and a method for testing them using survey data. It directly challenges purely economic explanations for populism, which sets up the puzzle I am trying to solve. Connects with Mudde (2004) on my list who defines the populist ideology itself.
Example B · Interpretivist — Wars and Women's Rights
CITATION:
Enloe, C. (2000). Maneuvers: The international politics of militarizing women's lives. University of California Press.

WHAT IT SAYS:
Enloe examines how states and militaries deliberately shape and exploit women's roles during and after conflict. Using a feminist interpretive lens, she traces how mobilizing women for wartime labor or service creates expectations that can either expand rights or be reversed once the conflict ends. Her method is interpretive — she reads policy documents, propaganda, and personal testimonies as texts revealing gendered power structures.

MY EVALUATION:
Essential for my interpretivist framework. Enloe does not use quantitative data, which means I cannot extract measurable variables from her work — but that is not her point and not my goal either. Her limitation is that she focuses heavily on Western and American cases, so I need to supplement with scholarship on the Global South.

HOW IT CONNECTS:
Enloe gives me my interpretive framework — I will read post-war policy documents and public discourse in both my cases the way she reads them, looking for how gender roles are constructed and contested. She directly challenges liberal accounts that treat women's rights as automatic byproducts of democratization.
Example C · Critical — Globalization and Human Mobility
CITATION:
Bauman, Z. (1998). Globalization: The human consequences. Columbia University Press.

WHAT IT SAYS:
Bauman argues that globalization produces a profound stratification of mobility — the wealthy and capital move freely across borders while the poor are increasingly immobilized and surveilled. He introduces the concepts of "tourists" (those who move by choice) and "vagabonds" (those who move by necessity). His method is critical-theoretical, drawing on sociology, philosophy, and political economy.

MY EVALUATION:
This is the theoretical backbone of my entire project. Written in 1998, so it predates the smartphone era and the algorithmic border control systems I want to examine. I will need to update his argument with more recent empirical work on biometric surveillance and visa regimes. Still foundational.

HOW IT CONNECTS:
Bauman gives me the critical vocabulary — tourists vs vagabonds, liquid modernity, stratified mobility — that I will use to frame my analysis of contemporary border regimes.
