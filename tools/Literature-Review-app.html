What Is a Literature Review?
Read this before you start writing
The Literature Review is a conversation, not a list. You are telling the story of a scholarly debate and showing where your research enters it. It is organized by schools of thought and arguments, not author by author.
A strong lit review: Groups sources by argument, not by author. Shows how schools of thought disagree. Takes rival explanations seriously. Identifies a specific gap. Uses transitions between schools.
A weak lit review: Summarizes one by one — "Author A says... Author B says..." Ignores rival explanations. Claims a gap without showing it. Has no organizational logic. Reads like an annotated bibliography.
Example: What "The Landscape" looks like ▾
Topic: Populism in Italy vs Germany after 2008

The rise of populist movements across Western democracies since the 2008 financial crisis has generated one of the most active debates in comparative politics. Why did some countries experience dramatic populist surges while others with similar economic conditions did not? Italy's Five Star Movement and Lega achieved governing power, while Germany's AfD remained a protest party unable to break into coalition politics. This variation in populist success under similar structural pressures is the puzzle that drives the present research. The existing literature offers competing explanations rooted in economic insecurity, cultural backlash, and institutional design — but has struggled to explain why these factors produce such different outcomes in otherwise comparable European democracies.
Example: What "Schools of Thought" looks like ▾
Note how sources are grouped by argument, not listed one by one:

The dominant explanation in the positivist literature attributes populist success to economic insecurity. Inglehart and Norris (2016) demonstrate through survey data across 30 countries that voters who perceive themselves as economically "left behind" are significantly more likely to support populist parties. Rodrik (2018) reinforces this with a political economy model showing how trade shocks and financial crises create demand for protectionist, anti-establishment platforms. However, this economic account struggles with the variation in my cases — both Italy and Germany experienced the eurozone crisis, yet populist trajectories diverged sharply.

A second camp challenges the purely economic story by emphasizing cultural identity. Mudde (2004) argues that populism is fundamentally an ideological phenomenon — a "thin ideology" that divides society into a pure people versus a corrupt elite. Norris and Inglehart (2019), updating their earlier work, find that cultural backlash among older, less-educated voters has stronger predictive power than economic grievance. This framework helps explain why populism takes different forms in different countries, but it does not fully explain why it succeeds electorally in some and not others...
Example: What "The Gap" looks like ▾
Despite the richness of this literature, a significant gap remains. Both the economic and cultural explanations operate at the level of voter demand — they explain why citizens want populism but not why political systems produce it differently. The institutional dimension — specifically, how electoral systems, party structures, and coalition norms mediate between populist demand and populist success — has received surprisingly little comparative attention. Inglehart and Norris treat Western democracies as a single category; Mudde's ideological framework does not engage with institutional variation. The few institutional studies that exist (Kriesi et al. 2008) focus on Western Europe broadly rather than explaining specific paired divergences. My research fills this gap by comparing Italy and Germany as a most-similar-systems design, isolating institutional variables that existing demand-side explanations cannot account for.

Common Pitfalls
The things students lose marks for most often
Source-by-source summarizing. If your Schools section reads like "Author A argues... Author B argues... Author C argues..." you are writing an annotated bibliography, not a literature review. Group by argument.
Strawmanning rivals. If your rival explanation section is one dismissive sentence, your reviewer will notice. Engage seriously — explain why smart people believe the rival, then show what it misses.
Ghost gaps. Claiming "no one has studied X" when someone has. Your gap must emerge from your literature review, not from thin air. Show the reader exactly where the existing conversation falls short.
