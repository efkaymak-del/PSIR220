Key Insight

There is no "best" N-size. A single case study done with rigorous process tracing can answer questions that a 1,000-case regression cannot — and vice versa. The question is always: what does your research question require?

Reading the Matrix

There is no single "best" column. Moving right gives you generalisability but costs you causal depth. Moving left gives you rich understanding but limits how broadly you can claim. Your paradigm track and research question together determine where on this spectrum you should land.

The Trade-Off (KKV's Insight)

King, Keohane, and Verba (1994) argued that all good research — qualitative or quantitative — follows the same logic of inference. But the tools differ. A positivist studying 80 countries and an interpretivist studying one speech are both trying to make valid inferences; they just operate at different points on this matrix. Neither is inherently superior.

Three Research Designs at Different N-Sizes

Each example uses the same broad topic — why states comply with human-rights treaties — but at a different N-size and from a different paradigm.

Strategist Large-N Statistical Study

Research Question Does ratification of the ICCPR reduce political repression in signatory states? N-Size N = 154 states, 1976–2020 (panel data) Method Fixed-effects regression with CIRI Human Rights Index as DV; controls for GDP, regime type, conflict. IV strategy using regional ratification waves. Why this N? The question asks about a general pattern across the treaty system — this requires enough variation in ratification status, regime type, and repression levels to isolate the treaty effect statistically. Trade-Off Strong generalisability but cannot trace the mechanism by which treaties change state behaviour.

Storyteller Small-N Comparative Study

Research Question How did domestic human-rights NGOs shape compliance narratives in post-authoritarian Chile and Argentina? N-Size N = 2 (Most Similar Systems — both Southern Cone, similar transition period, different compliance outcomes) Method Comparative historical analysis with process tracing; primary sources include NGO reports, parliamentary debates, newspaper archives 1990–2005. Why this N? The question asks how a process unfolded — this requires deep engagement with two carefully selected cases that share background conditions but diverge on the outcome. Trade-Off Rich causal insight into the mechanism but limited ability to generalise beyond the two cases.

Disruptor Single-Case Discourse Analysis

Research Question How does the UN Human Rights Council's Universal Periodic Review construct 'compliant' versus 'non-compliant' states through discursive practices? N-Size N = 1 (the UPR process as a discursive site); corpus of 120+ state review documents 2008–2022 Method Foucauldian discourse analysis — identifying subject positions, knowledge/power relations, and the construction of "normal" compliance behaviour. Why this N? The question is not about counting outcomes but about exposing how language constitutes the very categories of compliance. Deep immersion in one discursive site is essential. Trade-Off Reveals power dynamics invisible to positivist methods but makes no claim to predict or generalise.

The Lesson

Same topic, three entirely different studies. None is "better" — each answers a different kind of question. Your N-size follows from your question and paradigm, not the other way around.
