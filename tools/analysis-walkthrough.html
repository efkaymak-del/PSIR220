Set the Stage

Before diving into analysis, remind the reader of your argument. This is a 2–3 sentence bridge between your Research Design section and your Analysis section.

1

Present Your Evidence

What data did you collect? Be specific: numbers, dates, sources. The reader must be able to verify what you claim.

💡 Strong practice: "Eurobarometer trust-in-parliament data shows Italy dropped from 29% to 11% (2008–2013)."
Weak practice: "Trust declined significantly in Italy."
2

Test Your Observable Implications

KKV says: a good theory generates predictions that can be checked. List each observable implication and state whether your evidence supports it.

💡 List at least 2 observable implications. For each, state what you predicted and what you found. Use ✅ (supported), ⚠️ (partially), or ❌ (not supported).
3

Address the Rival Explanation

Test the alternative explanation with the same evidence. Does it fit better, worse, or equally well? This is the "differential diagnosis" — the step most undergrads skip.

4

State Your Verdict

Based on the evidence and the tests above, what is your conclusion? Choose carefully — and be honest.

⚠️ Never write "This proves that…" — write "This evidence supports / is consistent with / suggests…"

✅ Your Analysis Section Draft

Copy this into your Core Quest 4 paper and revise. This is a scaffold, not a finished product — add detail, smooth transitions, and cite your sources properly.

Self-Check Before Submitting

  • ☐ Every claim has a specific data point, number, or citation
  • ☐ Each observable implication is explicitly tested (not just asserted)
  • ☐ The rival explanation is tested with the same evidence — not dismissed
  • ☐ The verdict uses cautious language ("supports" not "proves")
  • ☐ Limitations are named — this is a strength, not a weakness
  • ☐ A reader could replicate this analysis with the same data

Set the Stage

Before tracing, remind the reader of your expected causal mechanism — the chain of events you hypothesized in your research design.

1

Establish Starting Conditions

What was the situation before your causal process began? Set the baseline so the reader can see what changed.

2

Trace Each Link in the Chain

Walk through your mechanism step by step. Each link needs its own evidence — a document, a decision, a speech, a data point. Name dates, actors, and sources.

💡 George & Bennett: "Process tracing attempts to identify the intervening causal process — the causal chain and causal mechanism — between an independent variable and the outcome."
3

Identify the Breaks

Where is the chain weakest? Where is the evidence thin, the causal step inferred rather than directly observed? Naming this is a strength — it shows analytical maturity.

4

Assess the Alternative Pathway

Could a different mechanism have produced the same outcome? Trace it briefly and show where the evidence favors your pathway over the rival's.

5

State Your Verdict

⚠️ A Storyteller doesn't say "hypothesis confirmed." A Storyteller says "the chain holds at these links, weakens at this link, and the rival pathway cannot account for [specific pattern]."

✅ Your Analysis Section Draft

Copy this into Core Quest 4 and revise — add citations, smooth transitions, and deepen the evidence at each link.

Self-Check Before Submitting

  • ☐ Starting conditions are clearly established with evidence
  • ☐ Each link has its own specific evidence (not just narrative)
  • ☐ The weakest link is named honestly — this shows maturity
  • ☐ The alternative pathway is traced and compared, not just dismissed
  • ☐ The verdict distinguishes between "fully traced" and "partially traced"
  • ☐ Another researcher could follow the same process with the same sources

Set the Stage

Remind the reader of your analytical framework — the discourse you're analyzing, what you expect it to produce and foreclose, and your positionality.

1

Describe Your Corpus

What texts did you analyze? How many? From what period? Produced by whom? Why these texts and not others? The Disruptor must justify the boundaries of the corpus with the same rigor a Strategist justifies case selection.

2

Map the Discursive Patterns

What recurring terms, metaphors, categories, or binaries did you find? Be systematic: use frequencies, predicates, or collocations — not impressions.

💡 Milliken: discourse studies must show patterns systematically. "The language seems biased" is not analysis. "The term 'irregular migration' appears 247 times with predicates 'managed,' 'combated,' 'prevented'" — that is analysis.
3

Show What the Discourse Produces and Forecloses

This is the core Disruptor move. What does this language make possible? What does it make unthinkable? What subjects, categories, or policies emerge from this discourse — and what alternatives are silenced?

4

Contrast with the Dominant Account

The Disruptor's "rival" is the mainstream reading of the issue. How does the dominant account present what you've analyzed — and how does your analysis challenge it?

5

Positionality & Verdict

The Disruptor names their position. Where do you stand in relation to this discourse? And what is your overall finding?

⚠️ The Disruptor doesn't "confirm a hypothesis." The Disruptor makes visible what the dominant account renders invisible. Your verdict is what the analysis reveals.

✅ Your Analysis Section Draft

Copy this into Core Quest 4 and revise — embed citations, add textual examples, and refine the analytical voice.

Self-Check Before Submitting

  • ☐ Corpus is precisely described — another researcher could assemble the same set
  • ☐ Patterns are systematic — frequencies, predicates, collocations (not impressions)
  • ☐ Productivity is named — what the discourse makes possible
  • ☐ Foreclosure is named — what the discourse makes unthinkable
  • ☐ The dominant account is stated and challenged with evidence
  • ☐ Positionality statement is included
  • ☐ Limitations acknowledge what your reading might miss
