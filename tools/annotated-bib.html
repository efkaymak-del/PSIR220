Positivist Inglehart & Norris (2003)

Citation: Inglehart, R. & Norris, P. (2003). Rising Tide: Gender Equality and Cultural Change around the World. Cambridge University Press.

What It Says: Inglehart and Norris argue that economic development drives cultural shifts toward gender equality through a process of value change. Using World Values Survey data across 70+ societies, they find that post-industrial societies show significantly higher support for gender equality than agrarian or industrial ones.

My Evaluation: The large-N cross-national design provides strong generalisability, and the use of longitudinal survey data adds a temporal dimension often missing in single-wave studies. However, the operationalisation of "gender equality" through attitudinal survey items may not capture structural inequalities. The causal direction is also debatable — does development cause value change, or do changing values enable development?

How It Connects: This source provides a foundational positivist explanation for my research question on gender policy adoption. It represents the modernisation school that I will contrast with constructivist accounts of norm diffusion.

Interpretivist Enloe (2014)

Citation: Enloe, C. (2014). Bananas, Beaches and Bases: Making Feminist Sense of International Politics. 2nd ed. University of California Press.

What It Says: Enloe argues that international politics is sustained by gendered structures that are rendered invisible by mainstream IR theory. Through detailed case studies of military bases, diplomatic wives, and garment workers, she shows how women's labour and experiences are systematically excluded from conventional analyses of power.

My Evaluation: Enloe's interpretive method — close reading of everyday practices — reveals dynamics that large-N studies miss entirely. Her work is persuasive in demonstrating the gendered foundations of international political economy. The limitation is that her case selection is illustrative rather than systematic, making it difficult to assess how generalisable her claims are.

How It Connects: Enloe provides the interpretivist counterpoint to Inglehart and Norris. Where they measure attitudes, she uncovers structures of meaning. This contrast is central to my literature review's debate between modernisation and feminist accounts.

Critical Spivak (1988)

Citation: Spivak, G. C. (1988). Can the Subaltern Speak? In C. Nelson & L. Grossberg (Eds.), Marxism and the Interpretation of Culture (pp. 271–313). University of Illinois Press.

What It Says: Spivak argues that Western intellectual frameworks — including those that claim to "give voice" to marginalised populations — often reproduce the very power structures they intend to critique. Using the historical case of sati (widow self-immolation) in colonial India, she demonstrates how both colonial and nationalist discourses silenced the actual agency of subaltern women.

My Evaluation: This is a foundational text in postcolonial theory whose central argument about epistemic violence remains highly influential. The dense theoretical language can obscure the argument, but the core claim — that representation is itself a form of power — is essential for any critical research design. The piece is more theoretical than empirical, which means it needs to be paired with applied studies.

How It Connects: Spivak's framework underpins my critical approach to analysing how development discourse constructs "Third World women" as objects of intervention. It provides the theoretical foundation that my discourse analysis will apply to contemporary policy texts.
