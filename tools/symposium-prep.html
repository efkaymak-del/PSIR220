Build Your 5-Minute Presentation

A symposium talk is not a summary of your paper. It's a compressed argument — the same logic as your abstract, but spoken and visual. Fill in each slide below. Keep text minimal; you'll say the details.

1

Title & Hook

~30 seconds

Your title, your name, and one sentence that makes the audience care.

2

The Argument

~1 minute

Your research question, the gap in the literature, and your core argument — in 3 sentences maximum on the slide. You explain the rest verbally.

3

Method & Cases

~1 minute

How you investigated. Name the method, the cases (or corpus), the data sources, and why this approach fits.

4

Key Findings

~1.5 minutes

This is the heart of your talk. Present 2–3 key findings. Use a table, a chart description, or bullet points. Be specific — numbers, patterns, quotes from texts.

5

Limitations & Next Steps

~1 minute

End honestly. Name one limitation and one direction for future research. This shows intellectual maturity — not weakness.

Prepare for Q&A

After your 5-minute talk, you'll face 3 minutes of questions. The best defense is preparation. Here are the most common types of questions at academic conferences — draft your answers now.

🎯 "Why these cases and not others?"

The case-selection question. Be ready to justify your choice with the logic from your research design.

🔄 "What about the rival explanation?"

Someone will ask whether an alternative theory explains your findings better. Don't be defensive — show you've thought about it.

📊 "How do you know X causes Y and not the reverse?"

The causality / endogeneity question. Explain the temporal ordering or your method's approach to causal inference.

🌍 "Can you generalize from this?"

The scope question. Be honest about what your study can and cannot claim.

🤔 "What would you do differently?"

The self-reflection question. This is where Pivot Badge thinking pays off. Show growth.

💡 You won't get all of these. But preparing for them will make you calm, confident, and articulate when the real questions come. The worst answer is "I didn't think about that."

Content

  • Research question is clear and specific (not yes/no, not normative)
  • Argument is stated in one sentence (not a topic — an argument)
  • Method is named and justified (not just "I used qualitative methods")
  • Cases or corpus are specified with selection logic
  • At least 2 specific findings with evidence (numbers, patterns, quotes)
  • Rival explanation is addressed — not dismissed, addressed
  • One limitation is named honestly

Presentation

  • Slides are ready (3–5 slides, minimal text)
  • Rehearsed at least once with the timer (under 5 minutes)
  • Opening hook grabs attention in the first 15 seconds
  • Closing line leaves the audience thinking

Q&A Readiness

  • Prepared answer for case-selection question
  • Prepared answer for rival explanation question
  • Prepared answer for generalizability question
  • Prepared answer for "what would you do differently?"
